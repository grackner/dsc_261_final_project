Topic: World - Australia

A devastating bushfire season continues to rage across vast swathes of eastern Australia as authorities grapple with one of its worst years on record for wildfher encounters and destruction. In New South Wales, the fire crisis has reached unprecedented levels, leading officials from Sydney Harbour down to Queensland's Sunshine Coast in desperate bids to control blazes that have engulfed hundreds of homes since September.

The most significant fires burning are concentrated around Australia's south-east coastline where temperatures soared and the dry season turned treacherous, causing uncontrolled bushfires across national parks, nature reserves, farmlands, suburban backyards, urban areas – nearly every kind of landscape.

Fire authorities have been forced to evacuate thousands due to rapidly changing conditions as they continue their relentless fight against what the Bureau of Meteorology has warned could be a 'once in a century' heatwave lasting through January and possibly February. According to Australia’s Chief Scientist, Dr. Pete Cilliers, "We are currently witnessing fire behaviour that is beyond anything seen before."

The bushfires have been responsible for the largest loss of life since Black Saturday infernos in 1939 when several dozens lost their lives to fast-moving wildfire and ash fall. Since September alone, over a dozen people perished from this year's fires across different states—a number that may still climb before they’re extinguished entirely by spring.

Dr. Cilliers attributes the unprecedented intensity of bushfires to human-induced climate change saying it "has substantially increased fire weather conditions and, in turn, created a more severe wildfire season." While Australia has experienced an increase in annual temperatures over recent years, 2019 was particularly catastrophic.

"We are likely living with the impact of these fires for several months yet to come,” says NSW Premier Gladys Berejiklian at a press conference on December 5th when she confirmed evacuation orders would continue in some areas beyond Christmas day due to “the very dangerous nature” of unpredictable fire behavior.

The disaster's severity has prompted international aid with several countries donating equipment, and experts from around the world offering advice on fighting these deadly fires while maintaining their own safety measures back home where they could face similar threats later in winter months if climate change continues to alter weather patterns. 

In response to the widespread devastation wrought by Australia's bushfires, environmental experts and activists have begun advocating for immediate action on reducing carbon emissions worldwide while demanding changes at national levels that will better prepare countries like Australia against these increasingly severe natural disasters. A recent UN report predicts a 37% increase in wildfire incidents globally by 2050 if current emission trends continue, highlighting the global threat of climate change and its impact on our environment today more than ever before.

Australian Prime Minister Scott Morrison has called for calm amidst reports of escalating chaos caused by these ferocious fires which have seen upwards of 12 million hectares (about 30% of Australia’s landmass) scorched, destroyed or damaged beyond immediate return. He assured the Australian public that “our government is doing everything in our power to assist those affected.”
