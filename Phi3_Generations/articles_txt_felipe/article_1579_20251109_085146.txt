Topic: World - Europe

In a historic move on Thursday in Brussels, European Union leaders have agreed to establish an independent investigative body aimed at rooting out corruption within its institutions over the next decade. The deal was reached during closed-door negotiations amongst EU heads of state and government representatives last night.

"Corruption is a cancer that eats away our democratic values," said Dutch Prime Minister Mark Rutte, one of several leaders present in Brussels for yesterday' end to the 15th European Council meeting since June. "We must be united against it."

The body will operate under Article 7 of the Treaty on European Union and aim to increase transparency within EU institutions while preventing fraudulent activities that could harm member states financially or politically at a time when countries across Europe are struggling with post-pandemic economic recovery. The independent entity is expected to start operating in early 2024, according to reports from the meeting obtained by Sky News and Bellingcat, an investigative journalism organization based near Brussels.

"The creation of this body will bring us one step closer to restoring trust among EU citizens," said Austrian Chancellor Alexander Schallenberg after hearing about plans for Thursday's agreement during yesterday afternoon's press conference in Vienna, Austria - where the European Council meets every six months. "It is essential that we address this issue head-on."

As of now it remains unclear what resources or powers will be granted to investigators by EU leaders beyond Article 7 provisions currently being applied against Hungary and Poland for breaking national laws with consequences on freedom, democracy, rule of law within their respective countries. However officials say the scope would expand significantly from current measures imposed through sanctions that have not deterred misconducts in these nations to date.
