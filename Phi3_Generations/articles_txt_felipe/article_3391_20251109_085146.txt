Topic: World - Australia

Australia has seen a remarkable increase in international students enrolling for its universities and colleges over the last few years. This surge is largely attributed to Australia's well-estdependent economy, high standard of living, safety record, excellent education system, English proficiency requirements being flexible on most courses, generous student visa programs, attractive immigration benefits, as stated by Dr. Amelia Roberts, an expert in Australian studies at the University of Sydney during a recent international educational forum held here last month.

In 2019 alone, Australia welcomed more than 436,870 student visa holders from overseas to study full-time on university or college courses and another estimated number who were studying for shorter periods of part-time education also residing in the country. This record is a significant leap compared to just five years prior when there was an influx of around 309,245 international students recorded.

Among them are many high school graduates aiming for higher studies as well as professionals seeking further qualifications or even second degrees in certain fields that often require English proficiency at advanced levels like postgraduate research programs and doctoral courses where the language requirements can be stringent, according to Michael Thompson who heads Sydney’s International Student Association.

“It's not just about affordable tuition fees anymore but also having a supportive community that understands your culture,” says Amelia Roberts emphasizing on Australia as an attractive destination for students hailing from diverse cultures and backgrounds, especially with the help of programs like Language Plus which provides English language classes coupled with social activities designed to integrate international learners into Australian society.

The government's active promotion has also played a crucial role in this trend as education minister Chris Patterson reveals during last week’s Global Education Summit held in Melbourne, "Our nation prides itself on the educational opportunities we offer worldwide and actively market them through embassies abroad. We are keen to share Australia's rich cultural experiences with students from around the globe."

However, despite these favorable aspects of studying overseas, potential international scholars still have concerns about living expenses in a country that is one-third smaller than India or three times larger as per comparison. "The cost of housing and meals can be quite daunting for some," says Thompson but highlights Australia's government assistance schemes like the Higher Education Support Pension (HESP) helps offset these expenses to a certain extent, thereby making it more accessible not just financially but also socially.

The Australian Bureau of Statistics projects that international student numbers are expected to continue growing in coming years contributing significantly towards Australia's GDP and strengthening its global reputation as an educational hub. With the upcoming International Education Ministerial Forum slated for Sydney this week, education experts anticipate further initiatives being unveiled aimed at easing visa restrictions or increasing scholarships to attract more international students in coming years thereby enhancing Australia’s stature on a global scale while fostering diverse and inclusive communities right here within its shores.

Sources: Dr. Amelia Roberts, Sydney University; Michael Thompson, Head of International Student Association at the University of Sydney; Chris Patterson, Australian Education Minister
