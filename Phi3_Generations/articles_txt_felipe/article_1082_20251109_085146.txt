In a surprising turn of events this past weekend at Citi Field in Queens, New York City, rookie sensation Jackson Smith shattered expectations by pitching an incredible five innings against the highly touted Mets-Yankees rival team. Born from humble beginnings on Long Island and rising through amateur ranks with impressive speed, 20 years old Smith stepped onto a professional field he never dreamt of just three seasons ago as part of his debut in Major League Baseball (MLB).

"I've heard all kinds of stories about rookies throwing five innings," said longtime fan Michael Rodriguez. "But I have to admit, when you see it unfold like this live-streamed on my phone – that took the cake." 

As Smith prepared for his debut in MLB against a team he's watched grow up next door since he was playing backyard baseball with friends and family as an eight-year-injured child, not many could have predicted this outcome. Despite being undrafted from high school ball—a rarity among success stories today—Jackson had always found solace in throwing a few quick innings at the local diamond after long days of recovery therapy for his injury that plagued him during adolescence, but never hindered it.

"I just love how baseball can turn life's disappointments into something worth cheering about," Smith shared before game day when asked by a reporter from the Brooklyn Daily Eagle last year after winning an amateur tournament in Flushing Meadows with his school team, The Rangers of Queens Academy. "Every time I get out there to throw those pitches and see people come up for signatures or just give me that 'Nice job' look – it means so much."

The Citi Field crowd held their collective breath as Smith took the mound in his MLB debut, hoping not only a potential career but also newfound inspiration would be delivered to those who watch and listen. The 20-year old threw nine strikeouts before being relieved by veteran teammate Evan Thomas after five innings—a time period exceeding expectations for rookie performance at such an early stage in their respective careers according to the statistics of baseball historians consulted with ESPN analyst Sarah Johnson.

"I thought he was amazing out there," said former MLB pitcher and Smith's mentor, now commentator Greg Allen after watching from his home broadcast booth for WPIX in Queens this weekend. "He took the mound feeling confident but calm – that showed real poise." 

The crowd exploded with applause as he exited onto the field between innings two and three, leaving a trail of cheers behind him while making his way to where Thomas stood ready in relief at first base.
