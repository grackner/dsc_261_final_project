Topic: Travel - Destinations

Travelers flock to Iceland in search of untouched natural beauty and unique experiences as tourism continues its upward trend globally. The glaciers retreat further each year has made the landscape even more accessible, attracting visitors from around the world between June and August—Iceland's prime travel season. In Reykjavik, local businesses are thriving on an influx of sightseers eager to witness the raw power of Icelandic nature firsthand while immersed in its rich cultural heritage.

"The impact here is significant," says Hanna Þórhildur Jónsdóttir, owner of a small guesthouse that has seen bookings double over the past year as visitors continue to be drawn by Iceland's pristine landscapes and geothermal wonders. "We are welcoming more guests than ever before."

Iceland remains one of Europe'thottest tourist destinations, with numbers projected to reach 2 million arrivals annually in the coming years—a figure that represents nearly a quarter of Iceland's total population and continues growth despite economic concerns. The allure for many comes from unspoiled natural vistas, geothermal spas amidst snow-capped mountains, bubbling mud pools with colors changing throughout the day, and hot springs as tall as two humans providing warmth against icy winds—Iceland's signature offer of earthly wonders has never been more magnetic.

But it isn’t only nature that beckons travelers to Iceland; cultural experiences have also seen a boost with an increase in traditional music and dance performances, as well as visits to ancient sites like the Viking settlement at Þingvellir National Park on July 27—a day celebrated for its national heritage.

Ice climbing guides report that demand has soared since Iceland reopened following pandemic closures earlier in the year, with families and individuals of all ages eager to explore outdoor adventure activities once again while adhering strictly to safety measures such as social distancing and wearing masks where required.

"There's a real sense of community here," says Magnús Sigurjónsson from Reykjavik Tourism Board, which has seen tour operators working around the clock since Iceland reopened its doors for visitors in June to ensure safe travel experiences throughout this period when COVID-19 cases have remained low. "People are more mindful about their environmental impact here and actively engage with local businesses."

Within a few short years, sightseeing tours by boat on the country's 20 lakes became one of Reykjavik’s most popular activities due to its stunning vistas. As Iceland continues to grow as an international tourist hotspot with nearly three million visitors last year alone—a figure more than double what was seen in recent years before reaching the pre-pandemic era back in 2019, which saw around two million arrivals and a booming economy powered by travel.
