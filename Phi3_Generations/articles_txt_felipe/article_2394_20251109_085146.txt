This extreme weather event is raising serious concerns about potential heat-related illnesses among vulnerable populations such as children, the elderly and those working outdoors.

In a statement yesterday evening from Governor Gavin Newsom's press office, he warned Californians to take this time of extreme weather "extremely seriously." He said that his administration was closely monitoring energy usage while also asking state facilities like government buildings and schools across the Central Valley to shut down non-essential operations.

The heatwave comes as California continues its battle against drought conditions, which have led some regions experiencing record low snowpack levels for this time of year due in large part to climate change scientists say. In Yosemite National Park alone last weekend saw 10% precipitation compared with what is needed at this point in the winter season; water reservoirs statewide are down about 74% from their historical averages for January, according to data released by California Department of Water Resources earlier today.

Dr. Rebecca Johnson, head climatologist at UC Santa Barbara spoke on Wednesday and shared her concerns over the impact this prolonged dry spell could have beyond just creating dangerous heat conditions: "This isn't an isolated event; these types of extremes are becoming more frequent due to climate change," she said.

Experts warn that if temperatures remain as high for several days, we may see a spike in hospitalizations related to heatstroke and dehydration among at-risk populations across the Central Valley region where approximately 7 million people call home; emergency medical services are being stretched thin already due to an increase seen over recent months.

California's National Weather Service issued a High Heat Warning for several cities including Merced, Fresno and Bakersfield today until midnight tonight because of "dangerously hot conditions expected within the next 24 hours" as temperatures continue to rise rapidly even above normal high temperatures seen at this time in May.

While some parts of California have received brief showers recently, overall rainfall levels remain well below historical averages for June and July which experts say will only worsen the situation ahead with forecasted drier conditions through September according to NOAA data analyzed by researcher Dr. Maria Lopez at UCLA's Scripps Institution of Oceanography earlier this week: "It is important that Californians understand just how vulnerable our water supply has become due to these dry spells in conjunction with climate change impacting the region."
