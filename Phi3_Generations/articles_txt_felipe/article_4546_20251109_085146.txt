Topic: Health - Sleep

In a groundbredependent study released yesterday by researchers at Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine in Baltimore, findings suggest that sleep apnea could be linked to cognitive decline and mood disorders like depression among adults over the age of 50. According to Dr. Helen Liu, lead author on this landmark study, "Our research indicates a strong correlation between poor quality nightly rest and significant reductions in executive functions and overall emotional well-being."

The team analyzed data from more than five thousand adults living across various urban areas in the United States. Using questionnaires to evaluate their sleep habits, coupled with comprehensive medical examinations including brain scans for signs of cognitive impairment and standardized psychological tests assessing mood disorders among participants aged 50 years or older between June 2021 and May 2022.

"One in five adults over the age of fifty suffer from sleep apnea, but only a fraction are diagnosed," says Dr. Liu about the findings which indicate that people with this condition were three times more likely to experience cognitive decline than those without any disruption during their nightly rest cycle. The research also reveals a significant connection between chronic poor-quality sleep and an increased risk of developing mood disorders, such as depression among the adult population in these age groups.

"This study is more alarming because it demonstrates that even people who feel like they are getting enough shut eye could be suffering from unresolved issues related to their quality or quantity," explains Dr. Mark Peterson, Director of Sleep Disorder Services at Johns Hopkins University Hospital and a senior author on the report published in the Journal of Clinical Internal Medicine last night.

Dr. Liu emphasizes that while more research is needed before firm conclusions can be drawn about this association's causal nature, she strongly suggests sleep apnea screenings as part of regular health check-ups for adults aged 50 or above to reduce the risk factors related to cognitive decline and mood disorders. "We need a better understanding in how we deal with such conditions," says Liu before offering some simple measures that individuals can take at home, including proper sleep hygiene habits like maintaining consistent bedtimes as well as reducing screen time prior to going to bed."

Experts urge those suffering from symptoms of poor-quality nightly rest or fatigue during the daytime to consult their physician without delay. "The earlier one addresses such conditions, especially in older adults where cognitive decline is a concern," adds Dr. Peterson about his hope for this study's broader impact on public health awareness and interventions around sleep apnea as well as other related disorders that can compromise quality of life among the aging population.
