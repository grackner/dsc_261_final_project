As authorities continue to probe a devastating landslide that struck rural Colombia on April 20th near the city of Neiva, an overwhelmed community still grapples with fear and uncertainty weeks later. In what experts are calling one of the worst natural disasters in recent memory, several homes were completely buried under tons of mud and debris when a hillside gave way early Sunday morning due to torrential rains that had been lashing the region for days prior.

"This landslide has taken so much more than just property; it's ripped apart families," said Maria Gomez, who lost her home and two siblings in the tragedy as she stood amidst rubble with other mourning relatives at a makeshift memorial site near their former community of San Miguel. "We have been survivors for generations here; we will rebuild together."

Authorities report over 12 landslides have occurred across several local communities this season with rainfall levels being up to three times above average for these parts of Colombia, according to government meteorologists.

"We are in a perpetual state of emergency," explained Hernan Perez, head of Neiva's disaster response unit, adding that the city and surrounding communities had been working together on relief efforts since initial reports came out early last week. "The mud is still flowing from one end to another due to heavy rains every night for nearly two weeks now."

In addition to landslides in rural Colombia, other nations across South America have also experienced intense weather patterns throughout the year that are causing concern among experts over climate change impacts on vulnerable communities. The UN estimates more than 70% of extreme natural disasters can be attributed to global warming-related events such as floods and landslides, a trend only predicted to worsen in coming years if emissions are not addressed quickly enough according to climate scientists around the world.

"The frequency of these catastrophic weather events is becoming alarming," stated Dr. Carlos Mendez at Universidad de Bogota's School for Environmental Studies, who has spent decades studying environmental patterns in Latin America and their impact on communities like La Esperanza. "We cannot ignore the fact that our actions are contributing to these disasters."

As relief workers continue efforts around Neiva providing food aid supplies water purifiers tents tarps for temporary housing while authorities investigate possible structural causes of landslide risk in areas prone to heavy rainfall and soil erosion, locals like Maria Gomez maintain hope that with the support they are not alone.

"We have each other," she said quietly as her family huddled together on wet blankets outside their makeshift shelter near San Miguel's memorial site last weekend evening lighting candles in remembrance of lost loved ones and neighbors alike amid a backdrop of tragedy still resonating deeply within this resilient Colombian community. "We will rise again, stronger than ever before."
