Topic: Travel - Food & Drink

A unique culinary festival celebrating regional specialties from across Africa is set to grace Johannesburg this July with a week-long event that promises an unforgettable taste of the continent's rich cultural heritage and flavors. The first ever African Gastronomy Festival will take place at Tshwane Convention Centre, bringing together food enthusiastmers from all corners for tastings, cooking demonstrations, workshops, panel discussions, and performances to honor Africa’s diverse culinary traditions on July 15-20.

The festival aims to showcase dishes made with indigenous African ingredients such as millet, sorghum, yams, moringa leaves, baobab fruit, honeybush tea and more – all highlighted through the mouthwatering creations of local chefs trained in contemporary culinary techniques. The lineup includes some familiar names like South Africa's own Masterchef winner Charlize Teignie as well as globally recognized talent such as Moroccan chef Youssef Azamoum, who was recently awarded the prestigious James Beard Award for Best Chef in North America and has been invited to teach at New York’s Le Cordon Bleu.

South African Tourism Minister Tokozile Maphirah-Mahola said that "Africa is known not only as home of iconic wildlife but also its incredible food culture, which this festival proudly celebrates." While speaking during the announcement ceremony in Pretoria on April 10th.

"I'm thrilled to see such enthusiasm for South Africa’s culinary heritage and couldn't be more excited about bringing visitors from around the world into our kitchens where they can experience authentic flavors," said Chef Youssef Azamoum, who will host a live cooking demonstration at the festival.

The event features an array of African regional specialties with over 150 different dishes and beverages from North to South Africa that represent not just food but history, social customs, rituals, identity, tradition, heritage - all on one plate! Guests can take part in hands-on cooking workshops led by local chefs like Zandi Mogale who will show visitors how traditional stews and porridges are made with authentic ingredients. The festival also features a series of panel discussions, including conversations about food security challenges facing Africa today as well as the impact that climate change may have on regional cuisine in future generations.

The African Gastronomy Festival is organized by Cape Town-based nonprofit organization AfroCulina and its partners: South African Tourism Board, Diverse City SA and The University of Pretoria (Potchefstroom Campus). All proceeds will go to the Foundation for Travel Development in Africa.

A representative from Mama Gravy’s Kitchen who attended as a guest speaker said that “cooking is my therapy, but sharing it with others and hearing their stories while cooking together has been an absolute joy.” As she explained how her grandmother used to make this famous South African stew in the early 20th century when times were tough for all.
