Topic: World - Africa

CAPETOWN, NAMIBIA – In what is being described as one of Africa's most significant archaeological discoveries in decades, a team led by Dr. Susan Kwame has unearthed an ancient burial ground containing more than 50 well-preserved graves dating back to the Stone Age on private land near Capeton Plateau this weekend (March 14).

Archaeologists were astonished at their find, which dates from roughly 30,00 extricating details about ancient lives and burial practices in Namibia. The well-preserved graves have been radiocarbon dated to around the same time period as other known sites of similar age across Africa but are considered unique due to the diverse range of artifacts found alongside them - from pottery shards adorned with ochre paint, jewelry craftsmanship indicative of a highly developed artistic tradition among early inhabitants, and an array of tools that shed light on their daily life.

Dr. Kwame said the find was "the most exciting discovery I've encountered in my career," while discussing it with her colleagues at Windhoek University last Friday (March 15). She added: “This site provides a rare window into our understanding of early human societies, particularly their burial practices and social structures.”

The Namibian government has immediately declared the area a national monument to protect against looting. The discovery's location near Capeton Plateau is significant as it suggests that sophisticated cultures may have thrived in southern Africa much earlier than previously thought, providing new insights into prehistoric human migration and adaptation patterns across this part of the world.

Eduardo Alvarez from the South African Institute for Archaeological Research said: "It's an unprecedented opportunity to expand our knowledge about early humans in Africa." 

As archaeologists continue their work, experts are cautiously optimistic that more secrets of ancient civilizations will be revealed. With a robust preservation plan now enacted and international cooperation on the rise for these kinds of discoveries across the continent, Namibia’s uncovering is poised to attract significant academic attention in years to come while providing hope as well - that more stories like this could emerge from Africa's diverse cultural heritage.

The government has pledged support and additional funding for archaeological research around the site, a sentiment echoed by many local community members who have expressed pride at their region’s historical significance: “We always knew there was something special about this land,” shared Martha Mueni from Capeton Village.
