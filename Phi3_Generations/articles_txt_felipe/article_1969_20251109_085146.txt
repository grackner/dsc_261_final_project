Hawaii Tourism Authority Announces Groundbreener Visa Requirement Amid Concerns Over Environmental Impact and Overtourism; Official State Rep. Kaleo Ana Hikialani Speaks Out  
By Jessica Nguyen, Staff Writer - April 4, 2023 (NewsCNN)
HONOLULU — Hawaii will introduce a groundnearing visa requirement for non-resident tourists starting May 15 in an unprecedented move aimed at reducing the carbon footprint and mitigating overtourism. State officials, environmental experts, and locals share varying perspectives on how this initiative could reshape Hawaii's future as a top travel destination while balancing ecological preservation efforts with tourist needs.

State Representative Kaleo Ana Hikialani explained the rationale behind the new policy: "We are at a tipping point, and if we continue to rely heavily on airplanes for transportation of visitors alone, it threatens our natural beauty that defines us as Hawaiians." He stressed during last week's press conference held in Honolulu that this initiative is part of the state’s broader sustainability plan.

"We hope travelers understand and appreciate these efforts to protect Mother Earth," said Hikialani, pointing out other tourism-related changes already implemented by local businesses such as eco-friendly practices at hotels and resorts across all islands of the state. "By walking more often instead of hopping into rental cars or planes, we can help Hawaii to maintain its pristine environment for future generations."

According to recent studies by University of California Los Angeles (UCLA), tourists who choose ground-based transportation rather than flying contribute significantly less carbon dioxide per person. The study found that an individual could reduce their annual CO2 emissions from air travel alone if they opted for alternatives like trains, buses or electric vehicles when visiting popular destinations including Hawaii islands; the report highlights these findings as a call to action against climate change by tourists themselves rather than solely relying on government regulations.

Hawaiian Travel Association President Leilani Keohokalole Iona noted that her members have been anticipating such changes for years, but said they understand the need now more than ever: "We're all about sustainability and taking care of our beautiful islands; we just needed some guidance on how to balance this with visitor demand."

CNN meticulously examined feedback from locals who fear a decline in visitors because fewer tourists can afford such expensive alternatives. One small business owner remarked, "It's important for Hawaii not only as an eco-tourism destination but also financially; we need to find ways that allow people of all budgets to come here and still contribute positively towards our islands."

Environmental advocates like Green Action Group leader David Kaimana laud the move, while emphasizing education on responsible traveling habits as a vital element: "Hawaii's beautiful environment makes it an extraordinary place for everyone – locals and visitors alike. However, without changes in how we approach tourism today, there won't be much to enjoy tomorrow."
