Topic: Science - Weather

A powerful typhoon made landfall in northern Japan yesterday evening with sustained wind speeds of over 150 kilometers per hour near Sendai City on Honshu Island. The storm was quickly named "Typhoon Kuro" by local meteorological authorities after it formed off the coast earlier this week and moved westward toward Japan' endangered communities along its path, with thousands evacuated to shelters in anticipation of heavy rainfall exceeding 300 millimeters.

Evacuees reported flooding as rivers overflowed their banks hours before the storm reached peak strength late into yesterday afternoon (March 5th). As Typhoon Kuro continues its slow movement across Honshu, it is expected to dump a further rainfall of up to 60 millimeters in Tokyo by this evening.

The National Meteorological Center warns that the storm could cause "moderate infrastructure damage and disrupt transportation systems," with officials urging residents on higher ground to remain vigilant as conditions deteriorate through tonight into tomorrow (March 6th). The Tokyo government is keeping its readiness for potential rescue operations in high gear, deploying flood barriers along the Arakawa River.

"The risk of further casualties and loss to property has significantly decreased since evacuees were ordered to leave their homes," said Chief Meteorologist Tatsuya Nogami at JMA headquarters. "However, we urge everyone still in flood-prone areas or along riversides not affected by the storm surge but that are already facing rising water levels."

As residents brace for extended power outages and limited access to supplies due to blocked roads from fallen trees, local businesses such as grocery stores have begun offering rationed goods. Tokyo Mayor Yuriko Koike warned citizens in a live broadcast yesterday evening: "I cannot stress enough the importance of personal safety; do not go outside unless absolutely necessary."
