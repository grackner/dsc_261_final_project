Topic: Health - Relationships

A study from Yale University reveals that couples who prioritize physical affection have stronger relationships and better overall mental health. The research team surveyed more than a thousand heterosexual couples in New Haven, Connecticut over the past year to assess how regular touch affects partners' emotional well-beth-ingness.

Professor Sarah Johnson of Yale University Department of Psychology led the study and found that "couples who reported higher levels of physical affection scored significantly lower on measures of depression and anxiety." The findings also suggested a correlation between touch frequency in relationships and reduced stress hormone secretion, leading to heightened feelings of safety and security among partners.

"I think this research underscores the importance that we take care not just of our own mental health but how it can ripple out into shared experiences," said Dr. Jason Kimble, a marriage counselor who collaborated with Johnson's team on the study in New Haven. "The more touch and affection people share within their relationship bonds, especially during challenging times like these pandemic lockdown years, the healthier we can all be."

Researchers found that couples engaged in activities such as cuddling while watching TV or holding hands on a long walk to boost intimacy levels and enhance mental well-being. The study also showed how partners who regularly communicated through touch during conflicts resolved issues more effectively than those relying solely on words, suggesting that nonverbal communication plays an essential role in strengthening relationships.

"I believe physical affection between couples is a cornerstone of healthy and lasting relationships," said Dr. Samantha Reid from the Department of Psychiatry at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine who was not involved with this study but agrees on its findings about touch in romantic partnerships. "Physical intimacy, when done rightfully is a powerful form of communication that can bridge gaps and bring harmony between couples."

As health experts urge people to maintain social distancing amid the COVID-19 pandemic, researchers are now reassessing how partnerships navigate physical interactions. The Yale study indicates relationships have been severely strained by prolonged separation due to lockdowns and limited contact with extended families because of fear for their health or safety during outbreak spikes in New Haven over the past year.

John Doe, a professor at City University London who studies human touch as part of his work on attachment theory said that while physical affection is critical within romantic relationships to maintain intimacy and bonding between partners - he added it's essential for both parents and children too: "Bonding through appropriate cuddling or holding hands with babies, hugs from loved ones when visiting friends- can make a difference in their development as social beings."

In light of these findings about the role physical affection plays within relationships - psychologists suggest that couples and families recreate touch during video calls through playful games or activities. The Yale team recommends simple things like "touching feet, hands, shoulders," to help strengthen connection between individuals who aren't physically together: a gesture which may bring comfort at such an unsettling time for many of us now that we are more than ever aware about the importance physical touch in our lives.
