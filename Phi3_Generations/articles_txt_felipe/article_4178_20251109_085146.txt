Topic: Health - Sleep

A groundbreener's night of sleep may come at a higher cost in modern times due to increasing levels of light pollution and sedentary lifestyles disrupting natural patterns that have long sustained the health benefits associated with restful slumber, experts say. Sleep researchers recently unveiled findings suggesting links between poor-quality sleep among urban populations and excessive artificial illumination in city environments where residents are exposed to more light after dark than ever before. In a study published last month by the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS), investigators assessed rest patterns using both self-reported surveys and wearable sleep tracking devices over six months among 500 participants across various metropolises including New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Houston, Phoenix, and Philadelphia. According to a survey conducted in January as part of the research team's ongoing work into urban environmental impacts on health: Sleep duration averaged at about seven hours per night for most subjects—an hour below optimal sleep durations recommended by doctors; however, nearly 20% reported feeling they had not slept well or rested adequately over the past month. "Our study highlights a growing concern regarding artificial lighting and its infringement on natural human rhythms," explained Dr. Amelia Zhang from NIEHS at New York City's Columbia University where she led one of three teams working alongside colleagues based out of Los Angeles, Chicago, Houston, Phoenix, and Philadelphia focusing on environmental effects influencing urban dwellers’ well-being—referred to as the Urban Sleep Health Initiative. Acknowledging that modern lifestyles have evolved with technology usage leading people indoors for extended periods at nighttime: "Humans are creatures of habit, and when our natural sleep schedule is disrupted due to factors such as light exposure or noise pollution—our physical health suffers," Dr. Zhang continued while noting that many Americans spend a significant portion of their evenings looking into screens with backlights emitting blue-tinted rays known for delaying the onset of sleep and impairing its quality when exposed to them before bedtime, especially during weekdays in cities such as New York or Chicago where nightlife is vibrant but work hours remain rigid. A separate report by NIEHS released last month noted that urban residents were more prone than their rural counterparts—especially those living above ground level like apartment dwellers and subway passengers, to experience higher noise levels from traffic congestion or crowded public transit which can lead not only to fragmented sleep but also increase stress hormones impacting overall health. Despite these findings that underscore the relationship between environmental factors in city living—and compromised human biological processes like effective immune response and cardiovascular functions, experts call for urgent public-private partnerships seeking innovative urban design adaptations to reduce light pollution through architectural planning with smart glass technologies limiting unwanted nighttime illumination. As cities continue their growth as population centers: "What we’re observing is a growing trend that requires immediate societal and policy shifts," said Dr. Emily Park, an environmental psychologist at the University of Los Angeles who specializes in studying how modern urban life affects sleep patterns but also points out solutions on adapting lifestyles to improve quality rest amidst this light-polluted setting as well highlighted during a press conference held last week
