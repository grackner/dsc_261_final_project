Topic: Health - Relationships

A recent study published in 'Journal of Psychosocial Research' highlighted a growing concern over intimate partner violence during long-term isolation due to pandemic restrictions. The report found an alarming increase in cases where one partner was physically abusing the other, with social distancing measures believed by researchers to be exacerbating factors that go beyond normal stress levels experienced outside of such circumstances.

"We saw an unprecedented surge of abuse-related injuries among married couples or those living together," said Dr. Thompson during a televised press conference yesterday afternoon (February 25).

During lockdown periods in which families were confined within their homes, psychologists witnessed an uptick not only in physical abuse but also emotional and digital manipulation cases among relationships across the state of New York. A recent survey by 'The Couple's Clinic', a nonprofit organization specializing in marital therapy, indicated that up to 30% more individuals sought help for relationship issues during quarantine than before March last year when pandemic restrictions began.

Experts weighed-in on the phenomenon stating "When couples spend so much time together without external stimuli and are often cooped up in a small space, tensions can escalate rapidly," said Dr. Robert Chen, Associate Professor of Psychology at Brooklyn College during yesterday's interview with 'DailyMail'.

The study also noted an increase among younger populations between the ages 20-35 who seemed to face unique pressures from work and home life balance disrufortuation brought on by lockdown restrictions. "It is especially concerning for this demographic," added Dr. Chen, as many are still in their formative years of establishing relationships while dealing with financial insecurities due to the pandemic-induced economic uncertainty.

Dr. Thompson added "We cannot afford a backlogged crisis with an expected rise of domestic violence as states begin reopening."

The New York state health department has pledged to conduct further research on the subject by extending funding for additional studies that investigate factors such as mental illness, substance abuse or unemployment.
