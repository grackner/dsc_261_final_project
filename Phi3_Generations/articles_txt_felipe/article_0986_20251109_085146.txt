Topic: World - China

A Chinese nuclear submarine is allegedly spotted near disputed waters in the South China Sea for intelligence purposes on February 10th by satellite imagery experts at SkyTruth, an independent organization focused on Earth's environmental health using technology to monitor global change and human impact. The presence of this military vessel within arm’in distance from Vietnamese-claimed Paracel Islands near the Gulf of Tonkin has sparked concerns over heightened tensions in a region where China and neighboring countries have overlapping territorial claims, as well as implications for regional stability and diplomatic relations.

Experts at SkyTruth analyzed imagery data from commercial satellites that typically monitor Earth's surface movements to identify the unidentified vessel resembling a nuclear submarine based on its size, shape, movement patterns, and water displacement indicative of such craft during sonar ping detection. "We initially spotted an anomalous object in our satellite data last week which we were able to further analyze as it approached closer this past Wednesday," says one expert at SkyTruth who wished to remain anonymous due to the sensitive nature of their work.

The South China Sea has been a flashpoint for territorial disputes involving countries including Vietnam, China, Philippines, Malaysia and Brunei among others since Beijing started its island-building campaign in 2013 along with establishing military bases on reefs and atolls it claims as Chinese territory. The waters are rich with fisheries resources that feed millions of people across the region while also offering strategic choke points for international trade, especially oil tankers transiting through straits between Asia's two giant economies: China and India.

The United States has conducted Freedom-of-Navigation operations (FONOPS) in contested waters near these islands under its "pivot to Asia" strategy but stopped short of naming the country that it saw patrolling this area on February 10th, citing diplomatic sensitivities. Defense Minister General Wei Fenghe confirmed China's military presence off Hainan Island last week when he told reporters: "Our PLA [People’s Liberation Army] has always been in the South China Sea and will stay there." However, no direct comment on this incident was made.

"The mere appearance of a foreign nuclear submarine near contested waters escalates tensions further," said Hanoi-based defense analyst Pham Minh Hoa who adds that Vietnam is concerned about the implications for regional peace and security as well international shipping routes being threatened by aggressive actions from China. In an official statement, a Defense Ministry spokesman emphasized: "Vietnam remains committed to promoting maritime safety in East Sea (Tran Bian) under cooperative efforts with ASEAN countries."

This latest development may further complicate already delicate negotiations between Beijing and other claimants including Hanoi, where the disputed waters were previously proposed for a code of conduct to prevent future conflicts. The United Nations-led talks have yet to reach an agreement on this crucial issue that has remained unresolved since 2014 amid heightened tensions after Beijing started land reclamation projects in these islands last year, which include military installations and infrastructure expansion within disputed waters.
