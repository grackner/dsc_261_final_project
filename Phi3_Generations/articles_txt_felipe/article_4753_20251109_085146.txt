A rare celestial event is set to grace Washington D.C.'s skyline this weekend as a total lunar eclipse will be visible in parts of North America and Europe on Wednesday night into Thursday morning (May 26-27). Weather permitting, millions across the region can witness an incredible natural phenomenon that has been centuries in the making.

The full moon is expected to reach a maximum point when it will be partially obscured by Earth's shadow as our satellite passes through what scientists call 'the umbra.' In some areas of D.C., residents, tourists and even day-trippers can expect upwards of 60% coverage at the peak phase with perfect weather conditions predicted along the East Coast from Washington to Boston for a limited time period around midnight local sunset on May 27th when this spectacs will be visible.

"Eclipses like these are truly special and very few people have been lucky enough to witness one in person," said Dr. Emily Roberts, an astrophysicist with the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA). "It's a reminder of just how vast our universe is."

This isn't your typical moon event as it will be viewed from two different continents simultaneously – Europe in London and Washington D.C., making for an exceptional sight, says Roberts. However, there are several factors that must align perfectly to make this celestial show possible: the Moon has to cross Earth’s shadow just after sunset; only at full moon phase can we see it all too clearly (which happens every 29.5 days); and our planet's rotation takes about six hours, which means the timing for observers on either continent must be exact as they look up into a sky still illuminated by daylight to catch this cosmic spectacle unfolding in real-time before them.

"I remember growing up stargazing with my grandfather and never being able to experience something like an eclipse," shares 25-year-old Emma Johnson from Alexandria, Virginia who plans on attending the celestial display at her local park wearing protective glasses provided by city officials. "There's just no replacing that sense of wonder as a kid."

While weather forecasts indicate clear skies along parts of America’s Eastern seaboard with temperatures hovering around 60°F, there is still potential for clouds or rain to spoil the sightings in some cities like New York City and Baltimore where local government officials have urged residents not turn out their umbrellas. The National Weather Service predicts a chance of scattered showers on Thursday morning with less than favorable conditions, particularly north of Washington D.C as temperatures can drop below 50°F in those areas which are expected to be cooler and overcast during peak times for the eclipse event making it difficult or even impossible to observe from vantage points further south.
