A deadly cholera outbreak has hit a remote village in South Sudan with over two dozen reported cases as of Monday afternoon and health officials are calling for urgent action to curb its spread. The small community, which lies along the banks of the White Nile River near Yei township, is home to about 300 people from different ethnic groups who rely primarily on fishing and farming with assistance provided by aid organizations.

"We have seen this outbreak before during other seasons, but never like it was happening now," said Dr. John Peter Ndege of the South Sudan Ministry of Health when speaking to reporthow the spread is likely due not only because communities in war-torn nations often lack adequate sanitation facilities and clean water supply systems (WHO link), which are crucial for halting further transmission, but also because many people live close together without access to proper healthcare.

In a statement released earlier this week on the same day as confirmed cases spiked in Yei hospital after visiting patients returned with symptoms such as watery diarrhea and vomiting that led doctors suspect cholera, Dr. Peter Ndege called upon aid agencies to prioritize improving hygiene conditions while providing immediate medical assistance for those affected:

"We cannot wait any longer; clean water must reach every family living in these camps without delay," said the ministry spokesman during an interview conducted via video call. As a result of this latest outbreak, one local fisherman named Simon Peter told reporters that "The river which brings life now causes our downfall as well."

Food aid distribution has been suspended amidst concerns over contamination while efforts are being made to transport water and cholera treatment supplies. The UN's World Health Organization (WHO) released a report earlier this month stating that accessibility problems persist due to ongoing conflicts, making prevention of further outbreak infections challenging:

"Access is our primary concern; we need unhindered movement for aid workers and medical supplies," said Dr. Amanda Jones-Cooke from WHO South Sudan office during an interview (WHO link). "Until the security situation improves, it would be impossible to provide sustainable solutions."

The United Nations Children's Fund also joined efforts alongside local healthcare providers in Yei township aiming at developing a long-term strategy for disease prevention and control: 

"We plan on implementing cholera vaccinations next year if necessary," said Dr. Mary Anderson, UNICEF representative based out of Juba (UN link). "Until then we focus our attention towards improving access to clean water as wells providing basic hygiene education."
