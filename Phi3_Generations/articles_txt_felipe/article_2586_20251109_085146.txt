Topic: World - Americas

During a stunning weekend in South America, Bolivia and its neighboring countries bravely faced an unexpected natural calamity that would go down as one of history's most astonishing meteor showers with visible effects on the ground — not just spectaccurally. Overwhelmingly bright streaks lit up skies over a 50-mile radius from El Alto International Airport in La Paz, Bolivia, to Corinto and Zaruma cities along Ecuador's coastline last Sunday evening (September 26). The U.S Geological Survey confirmed the occurrence of an intense meteor shower after satellite imagery captured multiple bolides — extremely bright meteors – plummeting from a single celestial source, something unheard-of in scientific history books as per Dr. Clara Rodriguez, Planetary Scientist at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and expert on meteor showers with over 20 years of experience: "In my decades working within the field I have yet to encounter a phenomenon like this; it challenges our understanding."

At first, many believed these streaks were part of an incoming comet. However, upon further analysis by Dr. Eduardo Gutierrez at NASA's Ames Research Center in Mountain View, they concluded that the bright lights displayed properties more consistent with a meteor shower than those of a celestial body coming into view: "Based on our initial assessments and comparing to similar occurrences before, it is highly suggestive we are witnessing an unprecedented natural event."

Authorities in Bolivia evacuated over 200 people from the path predicted by scientists for this meteor shower as a precautionary measure — even though there was no immediate threat to life or property. "We always have contingency plans, but never expected such an unusual event," explained Bolivian Meteorologist and Director of South American Sky Watchers Association Jorge Mendoza, who personally witnessed the celestial spectacle unfold in La Paz:

Meteorologists across countries that spanned from Ecuador to Peru continue monitoring for aftershocks but have confirmed they are stable with no additional risk. Experts like Dr. Eduardo Gutierrez and NASA scientists will keep watching as well, hoping the world can unravel this cosmic mystery together: "This may very well be a turning point in understanding meteor showers," said Rodriguez while studying night sky images from local telescopes across South America last Friday (September 28).

The aftermath of Bolivia's spectac0culous celestial display is yet to unfold, and scientists hope they can learn more about these bright streaks that lit up their skies in real-time. "In all honesty," Gutierrez concluded while looking at the sky with his colleagues on Saturday morning (September 29), "This truly feels like a once-in-a-lifetime experience."
