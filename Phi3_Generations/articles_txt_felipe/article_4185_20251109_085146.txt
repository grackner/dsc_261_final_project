A massive forest fire in California has forced thousands to evacuate their homes as smoke blankets nearby towns and cities since early Wednesday morning when it first started near Yosemite National Park's Wawona entrance, the largest of its kind for this time of year. The blaze quickly spread due to a combination of dry conditions brought on by an extended drought affecting much of Western North America, fierce wind gusts that exceeded 70 miles per hour at times last night according to local reports from Monterey County Sheriff's Office and the National Weather Service. As of today (Wednesday), it has engulfed more than 15 square miles but is still not fully contained, although firefigcurers have successfully protected numerous homes in some areas by building containment lines around them.

The California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection (CalFire) said there were approximately 20 structures threatened at its peak last night with the evacuation order expanding overnight from residents living immediately adjacent to Wawona, through outlying communities such as Grant Grove Village in Crane Meadow Parkway. Sheriff John King declared a state of emergency for Monterey County late Tuesday evening while air force firefighting resources were dispatched across the region to assist local agencies battling this fierce inferno, which has consumed nearly 15 square miles as it burns through dry brush and lush green forest.

"I just don't understand how we let things get so bad," said longtime park visitor Laura Johnson from Atlanta who arrived at Wawona with her family on Wednesday morning after spending time last weekend hiking trails throughout Yosemite National Park before the evacuation notice was issued. "The smoke is unbearable and I heard my grandchildren cry because their stuff got soaked in water from leaks as we're rushing out of here."
