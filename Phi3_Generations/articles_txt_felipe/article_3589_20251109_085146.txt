In a recent health scare that has spread concern throughout Southville and its neighboring towns in Pennsylvania, an outbreak of severe gastrointestinally illness attributed to contaminated produce is affecting hundreds this week alone. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) identified Escherichia coli O157:H7 as the culprit behind 23 reported cases of hemolytic uremic syndrome, a severe complication associated with food poisoning that can lead to kidney failure.

"The health and safety of our residents are top priorities," said Dr. Lisa Hensley, acting Health Commissioner for the Pennsylvania Department of Health in response to the outbreak which began on Monday near Southville's largest farm supply center at Green Acres Marketplace, according to official statements released Wednesday evening.

Symptoms among patients include severe abdominal cramps and bloody diarrhea; most had eaten produce from this particular source in the days leading up to their illness being reported on Tuesday night by a 52-year old woman, according to preliminary investigations. Officials have not publicly identified which specific food items were contaminated but warn of potential cross-contamination between products and emphasize hand washing while handling raw fruits or vegetables as an important preventative measure against such outbreaks in the future.

Local restaurants, including Southville Diner & Shrimp House where diners experienced similar symptoms last night, have been notified to ensure that they are following proper food safety guidelines and keeping their supply chains clean of potential hazards as much as possible under these circumstances - this has prompted a statewide review into current standards for safe handling in the industry.

At least five other cases were confirmed among patrons at Southville Seafood Palace yesterday evening, while an additional 10 people sought medical care today amid fears that contaminated seafood could be responsible given reports of fresh oyster purchases by some patients within a week prior to the outbreak.

The FDA's statement warns consumers against eating raw milk or unpasteurized dairy products, which have been identified as another potential source of E. coli contamination in several past incidents nationwide but are currently not suspected by health officials here; a local farmer and supplier at Southview Creamery assured reporters yesterday he adheres to strict safety protocols while preparing his artisanal cheeses for sale, though an FDA inspection is pending.

"It's devastating news," said Jane Roberts, mother of two who fell ill last night after enjoying fresh salad at her favorite diner in town - she recalls no recollection on whether they had ever heard any warnings beforehand about potential risks from consuming raw produce or even uncooked meat products back home where Southville's population originally hails, but nevertheless urges caution amid the unfolding situation.
