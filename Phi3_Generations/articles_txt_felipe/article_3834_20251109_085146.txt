In a devastating earthquake that struck Southern Sudan on Saturday evening near Juba—the capital city's largest urban center and home to over half of its population—several thousand people were affected, with the United Nations estimating around 50 fatalities. As aftershocks continue to ripple through the region days later at a magnitude no greater than 4 on the Richter scale, international aid organizations are mobilizing relief efforts and working closely with local authorities for effective response strategies amidst concerns about accessibility due to damaged infrastructure in this war-torn nation.

"The earthquake is shaking our already fragile country," says a Sudanese civil defense official who asked not be named, speaking from the city of Juba where tremors have been felt for days on end. "We've had little experience with such disasters here." The 6.1-magnitude quake was centered about 30 miles northwest of the capital and lasted approximately one minute before subsiding, according to local seismic experts at Juba University who monitor earthquakes in this part of Africa—an area rarely hit by tremors due to its stable geological location.

Reports from various towns along a 40-mile radius indicate damage varying across levels with some structures severely affected, while others managed only minor cracks and damages which are yet being repainidt in time as the rainy season looms ominously upon this war-ravaged nation. "There is water everywhere; it's not just people who need help," adds a local aid worker working on ground amidst severe flooding conditions reportedly due to heavy and unpredictable rain during these monsoon seasons in Sudan which are often accompanied by minor earthquakes that fail to make global headlines.

Aid agencies, such as the Red Cross Society of Southern Sudan (RCSS) and international bodies like UNICEF have already dispatched teams equipped with tents, blankets, hygiene kits, water purification tablets for drinking supplies alongside other essentials but are being hampered by poor roads in many areas. "We're trying our best to get foods and aid packages from Juba city center where we have warehouse facilities," explains an RCSS spokesperson who adds that efforts are ongoing amid severe weather conditions with the rainy season just around the corner, bringing even more challenges for humanitarian organizations already grappling with limited resources.
