This recent discovery has given scientists new insights into the cosmic nursery where stars are born within these clouds of dust and gas called nebulae. This nebula, which is approximately three light years in diameter and contains countless hot young stars glowing brightly against the backdrop of dust and gas, stands out due to its unique appearance - resembling an interconnected spider-web pattern with pockets representing embryonic star systems. The Hubble's observations have allowed researchers like Dr. Rosales-Barros and team at NASA Astrophysics Science Division in Goddard Space Flight Center (GSLF) to observe these processes as they happen, using the time dimension offered by capturing multiple images over days or weeks of observation cycles with Hubble's Wide Field Camera 3 instrument.

The latest observations reveal that star formation within NGC 6820 is ongoing and active; however, scientists believe it may have begun more than a million years ago when the nebula was first formed from shockwaves triggered by supernovae explosions or collisions between clouds of gas.
