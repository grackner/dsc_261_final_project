A powerful cold front sweeping across Texas caused widespin power outages and severe weather conditions on Tuesday evening into Wednesday morning in Houston, according to officials with the city's Office of Emergency Management (OEM). As many as 300,00 residents could have been affected by blackouts that hit various parts of Texas early yesterday.

The OEM reported seeing gusty wind speeds exceeding 45 miles per hour in several neighborhoods throughout the Houston area before midnight Tuesday when one local resident witnessed a large tree falling on her backyard deck, sending glass and branches flying into the darkness just feet from where she stood.

As if that weren't enough, an additional tornado touched down near Spring Lake Parkway shortly after 12:30 AM (CDT), taking with it another home entirely before dissipating over empty fields approximately two miles away by about 3AM; the National Weather Service had issued a flash flood warning for that area earlier today.

"It's been one of those nightmarish days here," said Houston Mayor Sylvester Turner during an evening press conference at City Hall around sunset, acknowledging not only hazardous weather but also potential water contamination risks as a result of compromised sewage systems statewide. "We are working tirelessly to restore power and clean up after these storms have passed."

Houston residents advised against unnecessary travel until late afternoon when officials announced most roads had been cleared, though debris remained scattered across many areas throughout the city following wind-driven destruction of vegetation. Power was reported restored within several hours in some neighborhoods while others may be without electricity for days to come pending repairs and infrastructure assessments undertaken by utility companies like CenterPoint Energy Co., which serves most customers living east or south of downtown Houston, as well as west-end areas including Beaumont.

City crews were still working into Friday morning with an extensive cleanup effort in progress involving hazardous material teams and specialists called on for assistance by other jurisdictions across the state; Mayor Turner said at least 12 counties have asked for help dealing with severe weather impacts, including Brazos County which reported two people dead from related injuries early today.
