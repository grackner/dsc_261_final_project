In a small town in Ohio, health officials have confirmed an outbreak of measles that began late last month when travelers from a country with high rates of the disease returned home to Springfield (population: approximately 164,000). As of this week, there has been one reported fatality and over thirty-five people are currently being treated for severe complications.

The Ohio Department of Health identified two primary cases among schoolchildren aged fourteen on both ends - youngest siblings to the same set of parents who had recently visited relatives in Nigeria where measles is endemic, officials say.

Measles is highly infectious; one person with the disease could, on average, spread it to 90 others.

Witnesses recount seeing children coughing uncontrollably in school hallways and parents rushing their kids off for immunization shots at nearby clinics as soon as possible after news of measles' arrival hit the town last weekend. As a result, Springfield has seen an uptick in appointments with health care providers administering vaccines to combat further spread within families and close-knit communities alike - efforts being backed by local government campaigns emphasizing its importance through educational outreach programs throughout schools across the town.

The Centers for Disease Control (CDC) has issued an advisory statement reminding parents that two doses of measles, mumps, and rubella vaccine - commonly known as MMR - provide lifelong protection against these diseases with "virtually zero risk" according to their spokesperson.
