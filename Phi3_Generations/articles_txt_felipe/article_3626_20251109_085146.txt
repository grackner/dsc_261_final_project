Initially hailed as a significant step forward for the league's global footprint and its ability to capture sports enthusiasts during off seasons or non major sporting events, current discussions now see NFL officials grappling not only logistical concerns but also local community backlash in Clark County. 

Clark County Commissioner Maryanne Terezmo expressed concern over the impact on casinos and public resources required to host an additional game per season: "The economic benefit we expected was outweighed by costs imposed upon our residents, from increased noise pollution during games till after-hours traffic jams."

While Las Vegas remains a primary destination for sports enthusiasts globally owing its vibrant casino and entertainment scene, the NFL's decision to introduce extra Wild Card game in an attempt at broadening appeal has seen significant pushback. The city is home to three of the eight NFL teams: New Orleans Saints (since 2006), Las Vegas Raiders (relocated from Oakland during their first season, 1995) and San Diego Chargers who moved back in 2017 after a stint at Los Angeles.

The proposed expansion would see the city hosting an additional Wild Card game per season with kick-off times set to coincide between midnight and early hours of morning, directly impacting local communities most notably around Las Vegas Strip where casinos stand in direct competition for limited resources such as conference rooms. 

Dr. Samuel Patterson, an expert on sports economy at the University of Nevada-Las Vegas shared his concerns: "The NFL is keen to broaden its appeal by expanding into international markets but they should first understand how their actions impact local communities." In a recent poll conducted among fans attending home games with over 7,000 respondents in Las Vegas alone, it was found that only around two-thirds of the participants were enthusiastic about hosting additional game. The remaining third expressed concerns regarding noise and traffic disruptions during weekends which could otherwise be spent at their homes or cities they reside elsewhere for a majority reason (48%), including business trips, vacations or visits to friends/family members in different states across the US.
