Topic: Health - Food

A new study released today from Johns Hopkins University found that certain diets can significantly reduce the risk of chronic diseases in adults over age fifty. The research conducted by a team led by Dr. Samantha Brooks surveyed more than two thousand individuals across various socioeconomic backgrounds and followed their eating habits for five years, providing valuable insights into dietary patterns that can contribute to long-term health improvements in this age group.


"We've found striking evidence suggesting plant-based meals with moderate fish intake are not only nutritious but also protective against chronic diseases like heart disease and type 2 diabetes," Dr. Brooks stated during her presentation at the American Heart Association annual conference last week in Chicago.


Participants who adhered to a Mediterranes-like regimen, which focused on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and olive oil while minimizing red meat consumption, demonstrated lower rates of hypertension and body mass index (BMI). The study also indicated improved cholesterol levels among those who followed the diet closely.


"The results are very encouraging," said Dr. Leonard Harris, a cardiologist not affiliated with the research team but familiar with the findings. "There's already substantial evidence supporting these eating patterns for health benefits; this study could push them to mainstream guidelines." 


Local food advocacy groups have taken an interest in disseminating the information, highlighting how accessible and affordable plant-based options can be with proper education. "It's not just about individual choices but systemic changes," says dietitian Emily Roberts from Green Eats, a non-profit organization promoting sustainable eating habits in Baltimore City since 2018.
