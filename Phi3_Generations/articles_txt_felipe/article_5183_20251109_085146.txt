Topic: Science - Weather

A powerful winter storm system has battered New England overnight, dropping snow accumulations of up to two feet in some areas and prompting numerous power outages across Connecticut as well as parts of Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Vermont starting late Friday evening into Saturday morning. As the blizzard continues its relentless march through northern states with sustained winds reaching 45 mph gusting up to 70 mph in certain areas, authorities have issued multiple weather advisories urging residents across a wide swath of regions from Maine down to Maryland to prepare for bone-chilling temperatures and potential travel disrupprtions.

According to the National Weather Service (NWS), snowfall totals near Boston were projected upwards of 2 feet in some locales, with accumulations expected to exceed five feet by early next week along coastal stretches before tapering off towards areas further from shore later this week. Temperatures are forecast plummet as low as minus 10 degrees Fahrenheit tonight across central parts of the Northeast and could dip even lower in New England's more northerly sections into next weekend, prompting concerns over frostbite risk among outdoor workers without proper protective gear.

Electric company officials have reported nearly 50,000 downed power lines since the storm began to move through southern states last night and predict it will take until mid-next month before all customers are restored in these regions across New England alone where over one million homes remain without electricity as a result of this historic snow event.

"The sheer volume, wind speed combined with drifts we're seeing here is truly unprecedented for our service area," said Patricia O'Brien from Eversource Electric in Hartford yesterday when asked about the current status on power outages caused by fallen trees and snapped utility poles. "We are working around-the-clock to clear lines and restore services, but it will take us until March next month."

New York State Governor Kathy Hochul warned residents across upstate New York yesterday as she toured a severely impacted hamlet in the Buffalo region where dozens of roadways remain impassable after being blanketed under several feet of snow, saying "It's going to take some time for us all just to recover from this disaster and that includes our infrastructure."

Local officials throughout New England have issued numerous warnings urging residents not only in their own communities but also beyond county lines who may decide come Sunday morning when planning necessary travel back home after potentially spending the past night with family or friends as they wait out the storm's peak. "It is critical that everyone stays off of roads until at least noon today, where possible," said Suffolk County Executive Steve Leans in a statement last evening while addressing emergency response crews across Long Island who have been battling through treacherous conditions to restore services since the storm made landfall.

Meanwhile officials from Connecticut Gov Greg Abbott's office continue efforts yesterday with state troopers and National Guard members patrolling roads throughout northern towns where snowdrifts as high as six feet were reported just this morning in some areas, saying "We are doing everything we can to keep our residents safe but they must heed warnings about travel until further notice."
