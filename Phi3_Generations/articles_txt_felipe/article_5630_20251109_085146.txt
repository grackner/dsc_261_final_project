Topic: Travel - Destinations

In a surprising turn of events this past weekend in Kyoto, Japan's famous Golden Pavilion was closed to tourists for urgent repairs that had been long overdue. The Shitennoji temple complex reported the situation on Saturday morning after identifying structural weaknesses during routine inspections earlier this month.


"The beauty of Kyoto shouldn't be marred by preventable risks," said Mayor Hiroshi Yamamoto, stressing safety over immediate public access to one of Japan’s most cherished landmarks. The repairs are expected to last several months with the pavilion remaining closed through late autumn at least.


Kyoto's Golden Pavilion – or Kinkaku-ji in Japanese – has been attracting tourists and locals alike for centuries, drawing an average of 20,000 visitors per day during peak season before the pandemic curbed international travel to a halt. Official statements now project that visitor numbers could be halved when Japan fully reopens its borders later next year due to ongoing global tourism adjustments.


Dr. Kenji Tanaka, an expert in cultural preservation at Kyoto University commented: "Regular maintenance is crucial for historical sites and this situation has highlighted the need for continual investment." The Golden Pavilion's closure also sparked discussions about balancing public access with conservation efforts.


Next steps include a revised timeline from local authorities, who are working to minimize disruption while ensuring thorough repairs and future prevention of similar issues at the site that has been an emblematic symbol for Japan's cultural heritage since it was built in 1398.
