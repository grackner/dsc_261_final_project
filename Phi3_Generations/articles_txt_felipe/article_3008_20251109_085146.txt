Topic: World - Americas

A powerful hurricane made landfall in South Carolina late last night as it rapidly intensified into a Category 4 storm within days of forming over open waters near Florida's coastline. Hurricane Zara, with sustained wind speeds reaching up to 130 mph (215 km/h), wreaked havoc in the Carolinas early this morning after bringing torrential rainfall and flooding through a region already battered by recent wildffer strikes. Emergency services rushed into action with evacuation efforts as forecasters warned of potential damages amounting to billions of dollars across coastal cities, towns, and farmlands in both South Carolina and Georgia. 

Emergency management officials from affected counties are working around the clock amid a backdrop of destruction left by Zara’s wrath: “The impact is just so devastating; it's hard to comprehend how much was lost,” said Gov. James Carter in an emotional interview with CNN, who declared a state emergency late last week after hurricane warnings were issued for the Carolinas and Georgia by FEMA’s National Weather Service office based out of Huntsville, Alabama on August 31st.

The massive storm system developed into Zara overnight as it barreled toward South Carolina with a life-threatening wind field that extended nearly three times the county's size – an area spanning roughly two million people in both states’ counties alone - and came just weeks after Hurricane Katrina anniversary. Local meteorologists said Zara was nowhere near as powerful or intense a system when it first formed, but rapidly gathered energy from warm waters off the coast before making landfall at 5:08 am EDT in Myrtle Beach – one of South Carolina’s busiest cities and tourist destinations.

“As soon as we saw this weather pattern emerging over Florida last weekend, our team started tracking it closely,” said Dr. Rachel Chung from the National Hurricane Center (NHC) in Miami-Dade County on August 29th who had been monitoring Zara’s rapid development for days and expected its strength to continue increasing as long as warm water conditions persisted over open waters off Florida's coastline - areas known among oceanographers as ‘hurricane nurseries.'

"The progression of this storm has caught everyone by surprise. Hurricanes in the Atlantic usually form during hurricane season, which goes from June 1 to November 30th every year," explained Chung on CNN Monday morning while assessing emergency response efforts as part of a joint operation with other agencies dealing first-hand impacts across multiple states’ lines. "As long as Zara remains over warm water and has access to the energy it needs, we expect her wind speeds will keep increasing."

Official statements confirm at least three confirmed deaths from fallen trees in several hard hit areas of South Carolina alone while tens-of thousands remain without power today with more than 30 reported flooded homes across coastal towns. According to FEMA estimates shared on Wednesday, potential damages throughout the Southeast could reach upwards of $15 billion and that number has not been revised since August when Zara first started its destructive path towards South Carolina after forming off Florida's Gulf Coast over 20 miles (33 km) from shore on July 17th.
