Topic: Business - After-Hours

In a dramatic series of events that shook Wall Street yesterday evening into early hours, an unprecedented tech stock mania led to massive trading volumes and subsequent market sell-off in the S&P 500 index on December 17th, according to sources close to the matter. The rush for high returns after traditional banking hours resulted in a staggering $3 billion being wiped from tech stocks alone by early today.

"The trading activity was relentless," says Sarah Collins, an experienced Wall Street trader who has been closely monitoring market movements this week. "Night-traders were making huge bets on the promise of high returns without considering potential risks." 

As tech stock prices skyrocketed throughout yesterday's after-hours trading period in New York, investors became increasingly fearful when reports emerged overnight that a major cybersecurity breach had hit several companies. Sources indicate the hack disrupted company operations and raised doubts about future earnings prospects for these tech giants whose shares have been soaring lately.

By 3:0in the morning, some of New York's most popular brokerages were seeing trading activity levels up to ten times higher than normal with trades happening as late as midnight when offices are typically closed in an effort to cash-out early and ride out any market downturn before regular business hours start.

"The surge of after-hours trade was unlike anything we've seen," says Michael Anderson, a broker at JP Morgan Chase & Co., "Especially with such high stakes being played on thinly traded tech stocks that are more volatile by nature." He adds the recent rally in these specific sectors had attracted many risk-takers who were not typically active during off hours.

Aside from technical issues arising due to an unusually heavy workload, Wall Street sources claim there was also a significant lack of transparency around some large trades being made that seemingly have no basis in the companies' financial statements or market forecasts for their respective industries – raising concerns among seasoned investors. 

"I can tell you this - it feels like we are witnessing something unprecedented," says Dr. Henry Kim, a professor of finance at New York University’s Stern School of Business who has been studying market trends for over two decades. "It's as if investors were blinded by hope rather than guided by reality."
