Topic: World - China

In a daring bid to assert regional influence in Southeast Asia on June 18th, Chinese submarine H023 surfaced near Borneo amidst the South China Sea territorial disputes. The event has sparked concern and heightened tensions among neighboring countries including Malaysia, Brunei, Vietnam, and Taiwan who claim parts of this maritime expanse as their own under United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).

The submarine's presence in disputed waters comes amid a backdrop of growing Chinese military assertiveness. The international community watches closely with an acute sense of wariness, given China’nergized claims over nine-dash line and expansive "nine thousand mile" maritime territory encapsulating the entirety of this contentious body of water based on historical rights dating back to ancient times according to Beijing.

“China's increasing military presence in these waters disrupts international norm,” said ASEAN diplomat Ambassador John Teng, who expressed his concerns about regional security during a virtual press conference today. “There needs to be dialogue and restraint from all parties involved.” 

This isn’t the first time this particular submarine has made headlines in recent months as it navigates China's expanding blue-water navy, signaling Beijing's intent of projecting power beyond its borders. Military analyst Dr. Liu Wei from Shanghai warns that incidents such as these risk escalating into military confrontation if not dealt with diplomatically in the near future: "The world is watching China’s actions closely, and a misstep here could lead to severe consequences."

As news of H023's presence spread across Southeast Asia last week, several nations responded promptly by increasing naval patrol frequency. In recent months alone since 2019 tensions have risen notably in the disputed waters – leading a significant deployment of foreign vessels to assert claims and protect interests - with both China's PLA Navy and ASEAN navies growing more assertive, according to research published last month by IHS Markit.

The South China Sea is one of Earth’s most heavily trafficked waterways – carrying approximately $3 trillion worth of global trade annually across a total area that makes up about 30% of the world's oceans and has been described as being “the next hotspot in international politics,” said Dr. Emily Wong, Senior Political Analyst at Singapore-based think tank Centre for Strategic Studies.

China maintains no official claim over all of Southeast Asia’s maritime borders; however its actions have sparked criticism from nations like the Philippines and Vietnam with whom it is embroiled in a series of complex territorial disagreements, further complicating already delicate relations within this region. The US remains committed to ensuring freedom of navigation but continues doing so through diplomatic channels rather than military confrontation as per statements made by White House National Security Adviser Jake Sullivan earlier today: "We've always been clear that we believe in a free and open Indo-Pacific. We don’t take the first step to an armed conflict."
