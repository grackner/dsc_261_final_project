Topic: Health - Relationships

A recent study published in The Lancet has shed light on how strong social bonds within relationships significantly improve mental health and overall well-betermindfulness of our emotional connections with others can have a positive impact, scientists say."We found that couples who reported higher levels of closeness also experienced less stress during the pandemic," explains Dr. Emily Tran, lead author on this groundbreaking study conducted in New York City over the last six months and based at Mount Sinai School of Medicine.[1]

The relationship between mental health and social support has long intrigued researchers but finding conclusive evidence remained elusive until now. The team surveyed hundreds of couples from various demographics across NYC, focusing specifically on the role that emotional closeness plays in maintaining wellbeing during challenging times like this year's pandemic.[2]

Tran says relationship therapists have taken note too: "It seems intuitive to think good relationships are a foundation for resilience. But now we can begin pinpoint where exactly those dynamics make the difference."[3] According to Dr. Michael Johnson, Director of Clinical Services at The Relationship Institute in Brooklyn, these findings could lead therapists toward new approaches when it comes time for re-evaluating relationship health during post COVID recovery periods.[4][5]

However, experts warn that while the study confirms social connections benefit our mental state overall — particularly among those already experiencing anxiety or depression – couples may need professional help dealing with unresolved conflicts before they can enjoy such benefits. "[If partners have ongoing struggles in their relationship], it's likely these findings won’t translate into improved well-being," says Dr. Samantha Lee, a clinical psychologist based at Harvard Medical School.[6]

As we move forward to reconnect after months of isolation or limited socialization due to public health measures in place during the pandemic — such as mandated masks and physical distancing between individuals—it becomes ever more important for couples, families, friends, and communities across America (and around world) begin healing together.

Reactions from readers were mixed when news of this study broke: "I'm glad to hear that there are positive aspects stemming out of relationships during such tough times," says Laura Simpson who has been feeling the impacts since early 2020.[7] On the other hand, some critics argue against focusing too heavily on finding solace in our social bonds when so many issues around healthcare remain unresolved globally.[8]

While not all hopeful couples can benefit from their relationship alone without seeking professional help—especially if they have underlying problems that need to be addressed first — Dr. Tran hopes people will at least consider the idea of reaching out for support when needed: "I'm encouraged by what I see here – even in challenging times, we find ways around adversity."[9]

For couples looking to strengthen their bond and better navigate difficult moments together — like any personal improvement endeavour worth pursuing requires practice. It may take courage for some partners when confronting emotional needs or insecurities within themselves but making time each day dedicated just thinking about your partner seems easy at first, yet it can often turn into such meaningful habits as sharing quality conversations over a cup of coffee.[10]
