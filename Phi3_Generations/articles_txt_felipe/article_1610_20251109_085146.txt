Topic: World - China

On Friday morning in Beijing, officials reported a significant increase in COVID-19 cases connected to Xinjiang'in wholesale market as of December 30th, with local authorities enforcing stringent measures and heightened surveillance amidst growing international concern. The announcement came after an outbreak traced back to the bustling central business hub in one of China’s far western regions has led global human rights organizations worry about the implications for Uighur communities already facing strict government control since 2017 when reports first emerged concerning mass detentions, labor camp sentences and forced relocations.

Official data shared by Xinjiang's health commission highlighted more than a hundred cases recorded over two weeks ago with infections increasing steadily despite initial containment efforts employing both technology-based tracking systems as well as human intelligence operations led directly from the capital city’s Ministry of Public Security. Local government spokesperson Chen Li stated, “The situation is being monitored very closely and we are taking appropriate measures to prevent further spread,” while adding that no restrictions have been put in place for daily life so far outside designated hot zones like markets or hospitals where Uighurs work predominantly.

Observers note this latest outbreak brings a different kind of urgency – not just managing an immediate health crisis but also preventing potential political fallout at home and abroad amid escalating criticism that Beijing's treatment towards ethnic minorities in Xinjiang amounts to cultural genocide. Human Rights Watch stated last week, “It’s clear China continues its systematic campaign of repression against Uighurs.”

The spread within the wholesale market has been traced back through contact-tracing efforts and initial interviews with infected employees who have revealed that they were subject to intensive surveillance since March 2019 when Beijing began rampant crackdowns in Xinjiang. Experts say these types of enforced regimens create conditions ripe for the spread not only because people feel uncomfortable discussing their personal health issues with authorities, but also due to constant monitoring and reporting on each other’s well-being via apps or cameras installed by management teams inside workplaces.

Shaikhan Asilov, a former employee at the wholesale market now working as an informant for journalists outside of mainland China recalls seeing Uighurs “always under close watch” while on shift; she adds that they would often go unnoticed or not even reported to management when feeling sick. "When I asked how we could report health concerns without being seen by surveillance cameras, the response was always ‘it’s none of our business,’" says Asilov, who has since moved back home in Kazakhstan after years spent within China's borders trying unsuccessfully to raise awareness about Uighurs there.

In addition to contact tracing efforts and enforced testing protocols already adopted nationwide following earlier outbreaks tied specifically with foreigners or visitors entering from abroad, the spread of COVID-19 amongst local communities in Xinjiang has added yet another layer for tension between Beijing authorities working towards eradicating a highly contagious disease and those who fear these measures are intended to further suppress dissent rather than genuine public health.
