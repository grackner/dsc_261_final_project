Topic: World - Australia

In a recent and devastating series of bushfries across southeastern Queensland, authorities have recorded one of the most extensive fires in decades that destroyed over two million acres as they raged uncontrollably last month (December). The Australian government declared a state of emergency on December 17th after multiple towns were threatened by rapidly spreading flames.

The bushfire crisis, which began early November due to an unusually hot and dry summer season in Australia, has been described as the worst environmental disaster for over five decades across southeastern Queensland states like New South Wales (NSW) and Victoria where most of these fires occurred. A total of 341 wildfires were sparked between November 25th to December 8th with several intensifying into "mega-fires" due to extreme temperatures reaching upwards of 90°F daily in the region, causing catastrophic damage and loss of life.

At least nine people lost their lives as a direct result from smoke inhalation or accidents related to fire evacuations while tens of thousands more were displaced by mandatory bushfire alerts across NSW's South Coast council area alone – an unprecedented disaster, according to Queensland Fire and Emergency Services (QFES) Commissioner Greg Byrne.

The fires have scorched nearly 15 million acres of national parks as well including the Great Dividing Range - home for thousands years worth heritage-listed plant species on their brink towards extinction with little to no regrowth expected in affected areas this season, according to ecologists.

"It's heartbreaking," says Professor James Smith from Griffith University who has been studying the impact of climate change on Australian biodiversity since 2015 - now witnessing first-hand destruction beyond his worst projections as he spoke at a recent conference in Brisbane, where researchers and experts have gathered to discuss urgent conservation measures.

"This is not something that will be easily recovered from; we're looking at years of regrowth before any significant progress can even begin," says Smith citing concerns for the Great Dividing Range specifically within his own study area, which has been identified as a key hotspot amongst multiple sites being severely impacted.

State Premier Annastacia Palaszczuk made an emotional appeal to residents and tourists alike on Wednesday (December 21st) while visiting the region with Queensland premier Andrew Lee at Sunshine Coast Wildlife Hospital where several species, including koalas rescued from perilous situations have been brought in for medical care.

"There's no doubt that this is one of our country’s most challenging experiences ever and we must all continue to come together as Australians united by empathy," said Palaszczuk during her visit, adding "The fires are not over yet but I can assure you Queenslanders will get through these hard times stronger than before."

As the bushfire crisis continues with temperatures still expected to soar this weekend and high winds forecasted in coming days across key affected areas - officials urge residents returning home after evacuating their properties not to enter uninhabited buildings due to possible lingering hotspots or electrical hazards left by recent emergency power lines.
