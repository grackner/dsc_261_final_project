Dam in Mali Gives Way to New Lake as Local Community Sees Hope for Recovery After Years of Drought  

By Aisha Idris and John Doe  

Published: March 15, 2024 - Tuesday Morning Edition

BAMA/MISANA, Mali (CNN) - The people in the small village of Babama have been living under an unrelenting sun for years. Now they look towards a new future as heavy rains filled what was once one of their dry rivers to form Lake Misana—a symbol of hope amidst drought and hardship that has plagued West Africa for decades.

After nearly three weeks, the village's main dam collapsed on March 12 under the strain of relentless rainfall, creating a natural lake within hours. "It happened so quickly we barely had time to react," said Amadou Toure, Babama’s chief engineer who supervised daily inspections before he resigned in despair after decades of failed repairs and neglect from the government-run water authority Mali River Dam Consortium (MRDC). "This could be a turning point for us."

It's unclear how many residents were affected by this unexpected event, which occurred near Misana township—an area that’s been on high alert due to persistent flooding and infrastructure issues over the years. According to MRDC officials who spoke with CNN at their headquarters in Bama city: "While we recognize Lake Misana is a relief for locals now after decades of drought, our priority remains assessing damages along Mali's vast network that supports millions."

Mother-of-four Fatoumata Sanogo watched from her home as the waters reached just above waist height during last week’s rainstorm. The 40-year-old schoolteacher shared: "We used to pray every night for rain, but now we celebrate together with our neighbors."

Experts like environmental scientist Dr. Ababacar Ndiaye believe that Lake Misana's formation could bring a longed-for change in local agriculture and ecology. As he explains: "Lake misana provides an opportunity for the replenishment of groundwater tables, which may help improve crop yields while also offering new habitats to wildlife."

The Mali River Dam Consortium says that it is working closely with local communities as well international aid organizations like UNICEF and WFP assess how best they can assist in repairing damaged infrastructure around the country. We must use this moment of joy for our people wisely, said Goudiya Yusufu, a MRDC representative who spoke at yesterday's press conference outside Babama township where officials discussed future plans to reinforce existing dams and construct additional reservoirs across Mali.

With an estimated 24 million Malian citizens reliant on Lake Niger for their livelihood—a source already strained from years of overuse due primarily by the needs of expanding urban centers like Bama, Misana's biggest city with half a million residents —the consortium says they have identified potential fundraising efforts that could be put forward through Mali’s national government to create an enduring solution.
