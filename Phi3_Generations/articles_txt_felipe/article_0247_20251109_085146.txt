Topic: Science - Solutions

A recent scientific breakthrough led by Dr. Emily Chen at Massachusetts Institute of Technology could change how we approach environmental solutions worldwide. In a study published this week in Nature Climate Solutions journal, the team has demonstrated that through genetic modification and selective breeding, certain microbes can enhance their ability to sequester carbon dioxide from the atmosphere—a promising step towards mitigating climate change impacts.

"The implications of these findings could be significant in helping us reduce greenhouse gas concentrations," said Dr. Chen during a press conference at MIT on Wednesday, marking her 15th year as an environmental scientist and leading the project for three years with international collaboration across multiple research institutions including University College London and Australian National University.

Microbes are known to play essential roles in our ecosystems by breaking down organic matter; however, Dr. Chen's team has now harnessed their natural capabilities towards addressing a pressing global challenge—sequestering excess carbon dioxide generated by human activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation processes that are causing an alarming increase in atmospheric greenhouse gas levels leading to climate change-related extreme weather events.

According to the research, specially engineered strains of bacteria were used on test plots within Massachusetts' Greenfield Fields where they showed a 30% higher uptake rate for CO2 as compared with their unmodified counterparts under similar environmental conditions. "The engineering techniques we applied here are not only efficient but also feasible in large-scale applications, which is essential," said Dr. Chen while explaining the potential of these organisms when used alongside other solutions such as afforestation and renewable energy sources to help create a balanced approach towards sustainability goals outlined by world leaders at recent climate conferences including COP26 in Glasgow last year; where nations committed themselves for reducing their carbon footprints.

"This research is not only promising, but it's also timely," said Michael Brown, an environmental biotechnologist and a professor of the School of Biological Sciences at Harvard University who was invited to comment on Dr. Chen's work after reading her findings in Nature Climate Solutions journal earlier this week. "We need innovative solutions like these if we are serious about curbing global warming before it accelerates beyond our control."

Although the study presented here does show promise, scientists emphasized that there is still a long way to go towards realizing its full potential as an effective climate solution; highlighting important research gaps in understanding how microbes interact with each other and their natural environment on such large scales. "This initial finding opens up new possibilities for carbon sequestration through biotechnology, but more work is needed," said Dr. Peter Smithson from the Australian National University's Centre for Ecology & Biodiversity Research who collaborated extensively with Chen during her three-year project at MIT; and where he now serves as head of environmental science research in Australia after returning home last month following his stint overseas to further expand on Dr. Emily Chen's pioneering work together for a more sustainable future, starting here on our beautiful Australian continent!
