Topic: World - India

In a bustling metropolis known for its rich cultural heritage and economic might, a rare celestial event captured millions of eyes across Mumbai yesterday evening as the planet Venus graced Earth's sky with an unmistakable presence before sunset. The sighting took place on Thursday, December 5th in India’s financial capital when locals and tourists alike gathered to witness this once-in-a-lifetime opportunity.

Experts from the Indian Astronomical Society have confirmed that Venus is visible for several weeks after sunset during its closest approach to Earth each year, but such sightings are rarely as clear in a city known more for traffic jams than telescopic sights on rare nights like this one. According to Dr. Ravi Shankar Agrawal, president of the society and an astronomer at Mumbai University’s observatory department, "This is truly exceptional because Venus typically appears as a bright star-like object with limited visibility in urban areas due to light pollution."

The celestial event not only drew crowds but also highlighted one of the most pressing environmental concerns facing India and its cities: rampant air quality issues. Amidst heavy traffic that day, citizens had gathered at Shanmukhi Park near Girgaum Chowpatty for a peaceful viewing session accompanied by live commentary from local astronomers – an annual event organized in conjunction with Mumbai's environmental groups to raise awareness about urban pollution and its effects on clear skies.

"The sky was cloudless, which is quite unusual for this time of the year," said Maya Patel, a long-time attendee who lives near Girgaum Chowpatty in Mumbai's densely populated Dadar area. "It felt so special to watch Venus right above us before sunset – I hope we continue doing things like this and remember the importance of our skies."

Scientists have noted that while celestial events often draw public attention, they also serve as a poignant reminder for environmental action. "It's ironic because it’s just moments after witnessing such beauty above us," said Dr. Agrawal reflectively to CNNMoney India when asked about the potential impact on viewers. “People are inspired and motivated, but also aware of our actions here below that impair these natural wonders.”

India's environmental policies have faced criticism for not being proactive enough in tackling urban pollution which has long plags its skies with smoggy haze during harsh winter months. The country’s air quality index (AQI) often reaches alarming levels, prompting the government to issue advisories and take measures such as temporary halts on construction activities when necessary – steps that environmental advocates like Avinash Singh from Green Mumbai have urged for more consistent enforcement.

"The sight of Venus was mesmerizing," said Mr. Singh in an interview with DailyMail India, "and it made me think about how we're all just tiny specks under a vast sky that can be so incredibly beautiful if only our skies were clearer." He adds: “I hope this event will become yet another reminder of the need for systemic change to tackle pollution.”
