Topic: Science - Solutions

A groundbredependent initiative that has captured international attention could transform how communities tackle flooding in Louisiana and beyond—an effort inspired by natural solutions but backed through scientific research as published on August 14th, just days before the historic rainfall event of Hurricane Ida. Researchers from Tulane University's School of Engineering have unveiled a new water absorbing pavement material that mimics how roots help infiltrate rainwater into soils and recharge aquifers more effectively than traditional concrete or asphalt surfaces, potentially curtailing runoff during severe storms.

"As climate change intensifies extreme weather events like Hurricane Ida—the costliest natural disaster to strike the United States in over a century – innovative solutions are needed now more than ever," said Dr. Lillian Chang, lead researcher of Tulane's grounddependent initiatives program and chairwoman for water management engineering at the university on August 15th after giving an interview with The Washington Post about their findings post-Hurricane Ida recovery efforts in New Orleans suburbs which were devastated by flooding.

The study details that when applied to roads, parking lots and other urban spaces where water accumulation is a common problem during heavy rainfalls or hurricanes like the one just experienced here (New Orleans), these specialized pavements absorb 90% more rainwater than conventional surfaces within mere minutes. This means they reduce flooding significantly while replenishing groundwater tables and thus, mitigating salt water intrusion into fresh water supplies which have been historically associated with the Mississippi River delta regions like New Orleans that often face such dilemmas even during non-hurricane times due to coastal erosion.

Civil engineering professor Michael Sullivan commented on these findings, stating: "I've spent my career studying sustainable infrastructure solutions and the potential for grounddependent initiatives like this one in Louisiana is immense." He noted that while more testing needs to be done before large-scale implementation across flood-prone areas worldwide - as evidenced by recent events affecting millions of people globally including hundreds dead in India alone – promising outcomes could save billions annually on disaster relief and insurance payouts.

"A lot more needs to happen before we can apply this technology across an entire city or country, but the benefits would be undeniable," added Dr. Chang who was quick to commend local governments for being receptive of piloting projects with Tulane's material within their jurisdiction as part of disaster mitigation efforts in partnership last month at a United Nations Climate Summit hosted by President Biden focusing on climate adaptation strategies.

"I hope that we can help demonstrate how embracing nature-based designs, which have always been around us all along but are now gaining traction after years of neglect due to heavy reliance upon hard engineering solutions such as levees and seawalls, provide resilience against future disasters while also revitalizing ecosystem services," she concluded in the interview.
