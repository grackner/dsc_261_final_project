Topic: World - United Kingdom

A major cyberattack has been targeting companies across Europe for months but now it seems to have hit mainland UK with full force according to official reports released by the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) on Thursday, March 3rd in London. The attack exploited vulnerabilities within thousands of businesses' digital infrastructries resulting in substantial data breaches and loss for affected companies since November last year without their knowledge until now.

As per the NCSC spokesperson: "This is a significant event impacting many UK-based organizations, disrupting essential services while causing financial harm." The attackers primarily targeted small to midsize enterprises (SMEs) in tech and finance sectors that often overlook crucial cybersecurity measures. 

The NCSC has identified a new strain of malware used for these operations, tentatively named 'EuroPhish'. This sophisticated piece is designed to evade traditional anti-virus solutions while remaining undetected on systems until launched towards the end goal - data exfiltration. 

A spokesperson from a victim organization in London stated: "We initially noticed issues with our networks back when it first began, but we put them off assuming they were isolated bugs and glitches." The CEO went further to say that since then the situation has only worsened causing massive disruptions.

Dr. Helen Carter from Oxford University's Department of Computer Science commented: "The EuroPhish is unique as it exploits unpatched software vulnerabilities which are surprisingly common among small enterprises due lack of resources and knowledge." The NCSC has issued a firm warning urging businesses to immediately update their systems while emphasizing the importance for SMEs' cybersecurity practices.

The UK government is working round-the-clock with international agencies, trying to trace back the origin of these attacks as well as identifying potential victims in order to mitigate further damages and restore affected services promptly.
