Topic: Science - Climate

In a recent development that underscries the dire impact of climate change on our planet's ice formations, glaciologists from Harvard University and NASA have made an alarming discovery in Greenland as they monitored with satellite imagery over four consecutive months beginning last July. The researchers uncovered multiple new lakes beneath a massive section of Humboldt Glacier that poses a significant risk to sea-level rise, according to the findings published this week in the journal Science Advances.


Lead author Dr. Elizabeth Wilcox said: "We were shocked when we first observed these subglacial lakes forming and expanding at an unprecedented rate." The team of scientists used satellite radar to measure changes beneath a section approximately 10 miles in width, where the average annual melting has increased by nearly four times compared to historical data.


"The acceleration is so rapid that it's clear we are not dealing with natural variability but rather accelerated ice loss due to global warming," adds Dr. James Hansen, a senior NASA scientist involved in the research project based out of Godthorgarsh Park near Harvard University.

"The situation is precarious," warns Hansen when asked about implications of this find on global sea levels and glaciology studies in general. "These subglacial lakes are now part of a feedback loop that will likely contribute to even faster ice loss." The scientists warn the accelerating melt could raise seas by two feet or more within their lifetime, significantly affecting coastal communities worldwide.


The glaciologists plan next steps in deploying new ground-penetrating radar equipment on site later this fall to monitor and predict future changes closely while international environmental agencies are calling for immediate actions toward substantial reductions of carbon emissions globally, highlighting the urgent need for climate action.
