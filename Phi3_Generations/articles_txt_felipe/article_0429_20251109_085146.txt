Experts from MIT who have advised on aspects of this project believe that with further investment the prototype could see spacecrafts traveling at velocities up to 25 times faster than current rockets, effectively reducing a round trip journey between Earth and Mars by weeks or even months.

Despite these promising advancements in rocket technology NASA's chief scientist has cautioned that "these breakthrough propulsion technologies will not alone be the key to our Martian odyssey." The agency also stressed safety measures, environmental impact assessments and political agreements as prerequisites for human exploration. 

As we look towards future space missions with this new technology at hand, NASA reaffirms its commitment not only in pushing the frontiers of science but doing so responsibly under international treaties that protect our celestial neighbors from potential harm caused by extraterrestrial activities.
