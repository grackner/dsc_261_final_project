Topic: Science - Weather

A powerful storm churning across South Asia is set to unleash torrential rains and strong winds upon densely populated areas in India this weekend. Meteorologists warned of potential flash flooding as the cyclone, known locally as Cyclone Titli, approached with hurricane-force gusts expected by Sunday afternoon near Mumbai'destreets and beachside homes on the eastern coastline around 4 pm local time (7 am ET).

Authorities have issued severe weather alerts for parts of Maharasthy state where more than a million people reside, with experts predicting up to five inches of rain in just two hours. The Indian Meteorological Department has urged residents along the coast and low-lying areas near rivers like Godavari Delta or Krishna River basin – already saturated from prior rains – to take immediate shelter indoors with no outside travel advised.

"As of now, our main concern is ensuring that evacuations are completed smoothly and safely," said Shyam Sundar Kulkarni, Director General at India's Meteorological Department during an evening press conference earlier today near Chennai where wind speeds reached 60 mph (97 km/h). "We ask all citizens to take this warning seriously."

Civil defense authorities in Andhra Pradesh and Odisha states have been swiftly mobilized with buses, trains and boats for evacuations over the last several days. Schools remain closed throughout Sunday while government offices will also shut down early today due to weather concerns along a broad swath of India' s eastern seaboard stretching from Tamil Nadu all the way up into West Bengal state near Calcutta, affecting at least 20 million people directly.

In Mumbai on Sunday morning while outgoing traffic was stopped due to floodwaters blocking some major arterial roads leading inland - particularly along Marine Drive and Girgaon Chowpet bazaars – emergency management officials are preparing for potential power cuts as high wind speeds could knock down trees onto overhead lines by early afternoon hours.

Power companies have begun rolling blackouts across the city of 15 million people, affecting millions with limited access during peak commuter times; a situation being exacerbated when temperatures rise on sunny days but cool off rapidly as winds pick up at nightfall – making for potentially dangerous travel conditions.

"I've seen some pretty bad weather before in my time here," said Sunita Kumar, 40-year old resident of Andheri East ward just northwest from downtown Mumbai who spent much of Sunday indoors alongside her three children aged four and under as they awaited an imminent evacuation order over the last few days. "But nothing compares to this kind, so I've been anxiously following local news since yesterday morning when officials first issued warnings."

Torrential rains began rolling into South Asia early Saturday with some areas receiving upwards of four inches per hour – a rate unseen in decades for many parts of the country already struggling through extreme drought conditions.
