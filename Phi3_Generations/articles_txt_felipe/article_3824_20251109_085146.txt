Travel: Disruption hits European rail networks as freight train derails in France
By Emma Thompson and Sophie Martinez-Diaz, CNN and The Daily Mail Reporters
Updated on Wednesday at 0937 EST (1437 GMT)
Paris - A major disruption hit European rail networks today after a freight train deracurity in France. Trains were reportedly stopped or delayed across the continent as crews work to investigate and address the incident that occurred early this morning near Saint-Denis, just northeast of Paris on tracks owned by SNCF RÃ©seau (SNCF).

As commuters rushed to their destinations for important meetings scheduled around 9:00 AM local time at railway stations across Belgium and Germany, a loud bang echoed through the quiet French morning. The sound originated from an industrial area where multiple railways intersect near Saint-Denis before branching out into different directions throughout France.

"We heard the initial boom that rattled our office window followed by tremors," said Pierre Dubois, a Parisian who commutes to Brussels for work every weekday morning via Eurostar and TGV services from Gare du Nord station in northern France at around 7:45 AM local time.

SNCF confirmed the incident when they reported that train A1802 carrying cargo had collided with rail infrastructure shortly before noon, causing damage to several tracks along a stretch of line between Saint-Denis and Paris's Gare du Nord station itself. According to eyewitness accounts from nearby residents living above the railway lines in Bonnecase, an early sound was heard at about 10:30 AM before they could see what had happened on site but sensed something amiss due to smoke or dust clouds rising towards their homes as emergency services rushed into action.

French Transport Minister Elisabeth Borne arrived just after noon at Gare du Nord station with her transport ministry officials and SNCF representatives present for the incident assessment briefing that lasted nearly an hour before they decided to evacuate all remaining passengers via bus shuttles provided by Eurostar until repairs are completed.

"This is obviously not good news but it shows we need robust safety protocols in place which can quickly adapt under unexpected circumstances such as this incident today," said Borne, who acknowledged the unprecedented disruption caused to thousands of rail commuters across Belgium and Germany's borders since France operates international high-speed TGV services connecting multiple countries on its continent.
