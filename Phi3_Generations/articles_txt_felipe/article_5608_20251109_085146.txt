Topic: Health - Sleep

In a recent study published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine, researchers found that adults aged 18-64 get less than seven hours of sleep on average per night over the past decade. The findings reveal alarming trends as experts warn about serious health implications if current patterns persist.
The study analyzed data from millions of people across various age groups and found that, in some demographics such as those working non-traditional hours or living alone due to the pandemic's impact on employment opportunities, sleep deprivation has worsened significantly. 
"We are facing an unprecedent health crisis; our bodies can't keep up with society’s ever-increasing demands," says lead researcher Dr. Maria Ramirez from the Sleep and Neuroscience Center at Stanford University, adding that a third of adults don't get enough rest to function optimally each day on average.
"Sleep deprivation affects our cognitive abilities as well as emotional control," adds senior researcher Dr. John Lee from Yale School of Medicine who co-authored the paper alongside her team, pointing out that lacking sleep can lead to an array of health problems such as heart disease and diabetes when combined with other lifestyle factors like poor nutrition or insufficient physical activity.
A 2018 survey found that around one in five adults struggles daily due to fatigue, making it hard for them to focus on tasks at hand including driving responsibilities where lack of concentration could lead people into serious accidents according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). 
Experts urge individuals concerned about their sleeping habits or those around whom they may feel worried - family members, friends, employers – start having conversations now that will hopefully bring change before it's too late. "If we want to improve our collective health and wellbeing, then as a society we need to prioritize rest," concludes Dr Lee while encouraging people in the community at large take actionable steps such establish regular sleep routines or seeking professional help if necessary – especially amid mounting reports of suicides linked directly with severe insomnia issues during these tough times.
Several local support groups have started organizing online meetups where participants discuss their experiences and provide peer encouragement in improving personal habits related to both sleep quality & quantity alongside experts advising on strategies for self-help or even recommending resources like apps available today that guide users through relaxation techniques aimed at better rest.
