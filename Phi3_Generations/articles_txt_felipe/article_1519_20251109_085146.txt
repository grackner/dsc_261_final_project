A bushfire crisis continues to rage through New South Wales' east coast in Australia as officials struggle to contain an unprecedented inferno that has torched thousands of hectares and displaced more than 1,00 endangered species native only to the region. The fire broke out on Tuesday near Sydney around dusk with a force so intense it quickly turned into what is now being called Australia's "worst bushfire in decades."

Experts have warned that the blaze, which continues to spread across vast tracts of land despite efforts by firefighters and local emergency services, is only set to intensify with worsening conditions forecast. This comes after devastating bushfires hit New South Wales earlier this year before moving southward towards Victoria where they continue to burn out control lines built months ago during the Black Saturday fires of 2009.

Dr. Jane Wilson, a climate change scientist at the University of Sydney said that "we're looking at record-breaking temperatures and prolonged dry spells which provide just the right conditions for this kind of disaster." With bushfires notoriously difficult to control once they reach their peak intensity due in part to fierce winds often reaching upwards of 100kmph, Australian authorities have been forced into taking extraordinary measures.

"We are doing everything we can," said Emergency Management Minister Mark Binsker as he toured a charred landscape near the township of Curradonga on Wednesday morning surrounded by flames that had already scorched 2,00 hectares - an area roughly half the size of New York's Central Park. "We have to act fast and make sure we protect people first."

Residents along evacuation routes were transported into temporary camps where authorities are working around-the-clock providing food, water, shelter; however their discomfort is not yet over as concerns about the spread of COVID-19 remain.
