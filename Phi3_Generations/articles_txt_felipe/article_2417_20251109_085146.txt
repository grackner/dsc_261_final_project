Topic: Health - Sleep

A comprehensive study conducted by sleep researchers at Stanford University has uncovered new insights into how poor night-time rest can lead to long-term health risks for teenagers in the United States, with alarming statistics on screen time before bed and disrupted circadian rhythms. The findings were released this week following a six-month observational study involving 500 adolescents aged between 13 and 19 across various socioeconomic backgrounds in San Francisco, where participants wore smartwatches to monitor their sleep patterns starting from January until July of the current year.

The research led by Dr. Emily Nguyen, a leading expert on pediatric sleep disorders at Stanford's Center for Sleep Sciences and Medicine revealed that more than 60% of teenagers exhibit signs of chronic sleep deprivation due to excessive use of smartphones before bedtime—a habit where screen time averages about seven hours per night, often leading to delayed bedtimes.

"Teenagers today are exposed to their phones far more than the previous generation," says Dr. Nguyen in an interview with CNN on Tuesday. "With constant accessibility of screens and social media use into late evenings, it's no wonder we see a disturbing trend towards insufficient sleep."

According to their study published this week, teenagers who used devices within two hours before bed had longer latency times (the time taken after lying down in bed until falling asleep) and took more time overall to fall asleep than those with less screen exposure. The findings suggested that bright light emitted from screens interferes with the natural suppression of melatonin—a key hormone for regulating sleep cycles, thereby extending night-time awakenings by an average of 20 minutes per hour spent on devices before bedtime.

"Our data show a clear connection between teenage screen use at night and altered circadian rhythms," says Dr. Nguyen's co-researcher, Dr. Mark Liu. "Sleep deprivation can lead to more serious issues like depression, anxiety, or heart problems."

In addition to the sleep study findings, a 2018 American Academy of Pediatrics policy statement highlighted teenagers' biological drive towards later bedtimes and wake times. The National Sleep Foundation reported in its most recent survey that adolescents need between eight and ten hours but only receive about seven or six on average.

With the holiday season approaching, parents are reminded of their pivotal role in setting sleep schedules for teenagers by limiting screen time during early evenings when sunlight is still present—a critical period that influences melatonin production and overall circadian rhythm synchrony. "We must prioritize rest to safeguard our children's health," advises a pediatric neurologist, Dr. Susan Choi, quoted in the study’s concluding remarks by DailyMail News Service yesterday evening.

As concerns grow among parents and educators about sleep-deprived teenagers being unable to focus or perform academically due to late nights spent on screens instead of studying for tests like the SAT exam scheduled later this year, initiatives are underway in local communities around San Francisco that promote digital curfew policies.
