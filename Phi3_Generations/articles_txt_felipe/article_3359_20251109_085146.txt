Topic: World - China

As of early Friday morning local time in Shanghai, authorities have implemented a new regulation mandating that all pet owners ensure their animals are microchipped by next year's deadline (December 31, 2thy). This move comes amid growing concerns over the increase in stray and lost pets within urban areas of China.

A statement from Shanghai Mayor Ying Yunsong read as follows: "The surge in unclaimed or misplaced animals calls for immediate action to ensure their safety, health, and proper identification." Experts suggest that microchipping will facilitate more efficient reunions between owners and pets while enhancing animal welfare standards.

Microchip implantation is a small procedure wherein the chip – about the size of a grain of rice - is inserted subcutaneously, usually in the scruffy area near an animal's shoulder blades. The device operates on low power and emits identifying information when queried by equipped microchipping services or devices at pet hospitals nationwide.

Sources reveal that many residents of Shanghai have mixed feelings about this new regulation, with some applauding the initiative while others express concerns over affordability and accessibility for low-income families who struggle to provide proper care for their pets in expensive cities like China's financial hub.

The city government has allocated a budget of approximately $30 million CNY (about $4.57 million USD) towards subsidizing microchipping services, especially targeting disadvantaged groups and the homeless pet community for compliance by next year’s deadline without imposing an undue financial burden on owners or shelters caring for these animals during their search for new homes.

A veterinarian at Shanghai Pets Hospital, Dr. Li Hua explained that "the microchip itself does not contain a power source and cannot be used as the sole form of identification - it simply works in tandem with collar tags bearing personal information such as names or phone numbers for complete registration."

Despite some initial hiccups, local animal welfare organizations have reported that early responses to this initiative are promising. In fact, a recent study found around 10% of the animals seeking shelter showed signs their lost pets were microchipped with up-to-date data already provided by owners prior to wandering off without them at large in metropolitan streets and parks where traffic accidents can be common causes for such distress.

Officials hope that enforcement of the regulation will see a similar trend nationwide, as it is set forth across other populous regions like Beijing, Guangzhou, and Chengdu under various stages leading to full implementation by next year's end. As this issue unfolds throughout 2023 - while pet owners adapt their furry friends’ routines accordingly – experts recommend continued efforts in education about responsible pet care alongside government-backed resources for those lacking means of proper identification before the deadline arrives.

Meanwhile, animal rights activists are urging authorities to focus on reducing stray populations through spaying and neutering campaigns coupled with strict enforcement against illegal ownership practices such as abandonment or unauthorized breeding that contribute significantly towards overpopulation problems faced by local shelters each year.
