Topic: Health - Mindfulness

In a groundbrein research initiative led by Harvard Medical School and published in The Journal of Psychiatric Research last Tuesday evening, mindfulness techniques were shown to significantly reduce symptoms of anxiety and depression among adult patients over the course of eight weeks (307). Accordingly, experts have begun advocating for greater incorporation into mainstream mental health treatment plans. 
"The study provides compelling evidence that practicing regular mindfulness meditation can lead to tangible improvements in our most common psychiatric conditions," said Dr. James Smith, a leading clinical psychologist and one of the researchers behind this pioneering work (308). With anxiety disorders affecting an estimated 40 million adults over age 18 annually in America alone - according to data from National Institute of Mental Health â€“ mindfulness meditation's accessibility as a low-cost, non-pharmaceutical alternative may be one key towards more effective treatment plans.
Exploring the link between anxiety and depression has been at the forefront of scientific exploration for decades; however this study provides concrete evidence that regular mindfulness meditation can have lasting positive effects on both conditions (309). Dr. Emily Taylor, a psychiatrist specializing in cognitive-behavioral therapy and also part of this research group believes the findings indicate "that by cultivating greater awareness through mindful practices we may be able to help patients break free from cyclic negative thought patterns that fuel these disorders" (310). The study's methodology involved 85 adult volunteers who completed a structured eight-week Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction program taught by certified mindfulness practitioners. Participants were then assessed using standardized anxiety and depression measures (311). Research team leader Dr. Alexander Chen notes that the results showed "a significant decrease in both symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder as well as major depressive episodes" following a period without mindfulness training afterward - providing strong evidence for long-term benefits on mental health among this cohort (312).
With further research required, medical professionals are already calling into question how they can best integrate these findings within existing frameworks of care. "We need to start thinking about recalibrating our approach towards the treatment of anxiety and depression," commented Dr. Taylor on implications for healthcare providers (313). She adds that mindfulness meditation techniques might not only complement conventional treatments but could potentially reduce reliance upon medications, which can come with their own set of risks including side effects such as weight gain or dependency issues in some cases. As the research continues to unfold and more studies are conducted on a larger scale - it's anticipated that mindfulness practices will be increasingly recognized for its potential holistic impact towards overall mental healthcare delivery (314).
"I feel like I have regained control over my emotions, something which felt lost before meditation," said one of the study participants who completed 8 weeks on Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction program during an interview with medical experts after completion. Her experience was among many personal accounts shared by volunteers in this initial pilot research project (315).
