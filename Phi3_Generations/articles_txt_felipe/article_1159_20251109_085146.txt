Gold Mining Accident in China: Officials Investigate Death Toll Reported at Approximately Five Hundred Markham Mine Last Weekend  

BEIJING - At least five hundred miners are reported missing following a catastrophic accident that occurred on Saturday afternoon at the ageing gold mines near the town of Markham in Yunnan province, China's state media Xinhua News Agency reports. The National Mine Safety Administration has confirmed an extensive search is underway for survivors amidst growing concern over worker welfare and miner safety conditions across many regions within Asia’s manufacturing powerhouse nation as it strives to become a global leader in high-tech production, according to the official English language Chinese daily Global Times.  

The accident occurred at approximately 2 pm local time when an explosion ripped through one of Markham's deepest mines located about three kilometers underground on its southwest wall near Kunming city center where it is notoriously cold and damp during the winter months, per a representative from China National Mining Association. Authorities confirmed that initial assessments indicate there could be as many as two hundred of those believed dead inside Markham mine at any given time with rescue efforts ongoing through Monday evening when conditions are expected to become too dangerous for rescuers due to high winds and risk of aftershocks in the region, reports China Daily.   
 
The Yunnan provincial government has since announced plans yesterday afternoon that a dedicated team consisting mostly former mining personnel will be mobilized with heavy equipment at hand beginning tomorrow morning as part of an intensive rescue operation aimed primarily but not exclusively towards those who are unaccounted for, reported Xinhua. “We cannot guarantee the lives or safety of our rescuers nor can we provide a timeline on when everyone might be found,” stated Yunnan government spokesman Zhao Jianping in an interview with China Central Television (CCTV) yesterday afternoon while visiting Markham, which is home to some 250 gold mines across its vast expanse spread between the Chinese territory of Xishuangbanna and Laos’ Phongsali Province. “This accident has shone a glaring light on just how dire conditions have become within our mining industry as it grows rapidly.”  

During his recent visit to Markham, Zhao told CCTV that approximately 80% of the mines in Yunnan are considered outdated by standards now but still heavily contribute towards gold production for both domestic consumption and export. “The state must prioritize our miners’ safety before pushing forward with plans aiming at modernizing these facilities,” Zhao continued while speaking to reporters from Phongsali Province yesterday, noting that his government is working closely together nationally as well regionally within the Mekong River area along Yunnan's northern border. “We are doing everything in our power towards bringing about positive change for those who labor deep underground where conditions can be unforgiving,” Zhao said while speaking to repor01
