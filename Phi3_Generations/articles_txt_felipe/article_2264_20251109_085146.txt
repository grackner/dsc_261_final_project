Astronomers have discovered a new celestial body in our solar system that appears to challenge previously held theories of planetary formation and dynamics. This groundbreaking finding came about when researchers at the European Space Agency (ESA) conducted an intensive search with their latest deep-space telescopes, leading them to observe what appeared to be a dwarf planet orbiting between Mars and Jupiter since December 2021.

Named 'Kerguelen,' after French seismologist Bernard Kerguelen who explored the islands nearby in Australia during his voyages on Earth's southern hemisphere, this peculiar object showcases an unusual elliptical orbit that defies traditional models of how planets move within our solar system.

Dr. Helena Weber, a lead astrophysicist involved with ESA’s project stated: "Kerguelen is not only the first new dwarf planet to be discovered in this region for over two centuries but also brings us an opportunity to expand and refine our understanding of how planets evolve."

Located at a distance from Earth, about 2.5 astronomical units away - which corresponds roughly with one-third of the average distance between the Sun and Planet Mars – Kerguelen has intrigued scientists due to its unusual properties that seem inconsistent with current scientific knowledge on planetary formation theories established during our solar system's birth, nearly 4.6 billion years ago when a protoplanetary disk encircled young sun in the Milky Way galaxy about six million times before planets began forming from it at present day locations and speeds they maintain today (according to NASA).

Scientists suspect that Kerguelen may have been formed through different mechanisms as compared with other known dwarf planet discoveries, such as Pluto. Dr. Rajiv Patel, a geophysicist at Cambridge University in the UK said: "Unlike more conventional objects of our solar system which typically exhibit smooth and almost spherical shapes due to their gravitational pull causing them to squish themselves into rounder figures over time — Kerguelen seems irregularly elongated. This suggests that something unique about its initial formation may have occurred."

Kerguelen's unusual orbit also presents unanswered questions, as it orbits the sun at an erratic pace with a period of 16 Earth years but shows no signs of tilting on its axis which usually results in seasons. This further complicates understanding how planets form and why Kerguelen might display such peculiar characteristics compared to similar objects found elsewhere within our solar system, as per ESA officials: "It remains challenging for us to fully understand the nature of this object due to limited direct observational time with telescopes on Earth but we hope that further study using upcoming space missions like NASA’s Lucy mission will help shed light."

Experts believe Kerguelen might provide invaluable clues about unexplored aspects of our solar system's history, as well as the processes involved with planetary evolution.
