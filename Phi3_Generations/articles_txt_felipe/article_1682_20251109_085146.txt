The Tiananmen Square demonstration marked a solemn gathering for activists, historians, students, and relatives looking back on the June Fourth Movement in 1989 when prodemocracy protesters were met with brutal force by security forces of then-Chairman Deng Xiaoping's government. As they marched under clear blue skies yesterday afternoon commemorating that day, a spokesperson for the Chinese Communist Party assured state media it was "not meant to undermine national unity."

Amidst this backdrop of remembrance and reflection on past turmoil in China's capital region sits an economic landscape seemingly untouched by such political strife. A survey conducted last month showed that Beijing is experiencing robust growth, with GDP increasing at a year-on-year pace of 7.5% as the city continues its push towards becoming "a world-class metropolis."

Official statements issued from Beijing's central government echoed this sentiment as Premier Li Keqiang stated yesterday during a state media conference: "As we face anniversaries of historical events within our country and elsewhere, I believe the best way forward is to focus on constructive dialogue that brings people together."

Several foreign journalists were present for Beijing's annual memorial ceremony held in Tiananmen Square yesterday. One journalist with The New York Times noted: "It was a powerful day of emotion and remembrance, but also one where Chinese citizens are proud to live under their government."
